# Handling the money: payment services regulation and client money

Researched: 2026-07-08, prepares solicitor questions, NOT legal advice.

Scenario analysed: client pays for booked care hours through the platform; carer sets the hourly rate; platform retains a 15% carer-side commission plus a 6% client-side fee and pays the balance to the carer via Stripe Connect.

## (a) Payment Services Regulations 2017 and the commercial agent exclusion

- Anyone providing "payment services" in the UK as a regular occupation or business activity must be authorised or registered by the FCA unless excluded or exempt [source: https://www.fca.org.uk/publication/finalised-guidance/fca-approach-payment-services-electronic-money-2017.pdf, para 3.3]. Payment services are listed in Schedule 1 Part 1 of the PSRs 2017 (SI 2017/752) and include "the execution of payment transactions" (para 1(c)), "issuing payment instruments or acquiring payment transactions" (para 1(e)) and "money remittance" (para 1(f)) [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2017/752/schedule/1]. A platform that collected clients' money into its own bank account and paid carers out would, on its face, be doing one of these.
- The commercial agent exclusion is Schedule 1 Part 2 paragraph 2(b): excluded are "payment transactions between the payer and the payee through a commercial agent authorised in an agreement to negotiate or conclude the sale or purchase of goods or services on behalf of either the payer or the payee but not both" [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2017/752/schedule/1].
- FCA guidance: three conditions must all be met (transactions through a commercial agent; formal authority to negotiate or conclude the sale; acting for payer or payee "but not both parties"). The FCA warns: "If a business acts on behalf of both the payer and payee then it is likely to require our authorisation or registration", and: "It is likely that a business will be acting for both the payer and the payee if payments are transferred into an account that it controls or manages before being sent to the payee, but the payer's debt is only settled once the payee has received payment" [source: https://www.fca.org.uk/firms/commercial-agent-exclusion-cae]. The same analysis, with marketplace examples, is in PERG 15.5 [source: https://handbook.fca.org.uk/handbook/PERG/15/5.html].
- The narrowing is a PSD2 change: under PSD1 the exemption was applied inconsistently across member states; PSD2 confined it to agents acting "on behalf of only the payer or only the payee", and a platform acting for both sides can only avoid a licensing requirement if it "does not at any time enter into possession or control of client funds" [source: https://stripe.com/guides/how-psd2-impacts-marketplaces-and-platforms].
- Applied to us: we take a fee from both sides (15% carer, 6% client), contract with both, and would arrange the transaction for both. We should assume the commercial agent exclusion is NOT available to us. That only matters if we ever possess or control the funds, which is what Stripe Connect is designed to prevent.

## (b) How Stripe Connect changes the analysis

- Stripe's regulated UK entity, Stripe Payments UK Limited, is authorised by the FCA as an electronic money institution (FRN 900461) [source: https://stripe.com/legal/spukl]. Stripe contracts with both the platform and the carer (connected account) and settles payments; Stripe's stated position: "Funds that are owed by end customers to the user (payments to the user) are never in the possession or control of the platform. Instead, these funds are settled to Stripe's safeguarding accounts", and this applies across Connect charge structures. "Marketplaces require a PSD2 licence (or exemption) or must be appointed as a PSD2 agent only if they are providing regulated payment services" [source: https://stripe.com/guides/frequently-asked-questions-about-stripe-connect-and-psd2].
- Charge types [source: https://docs.stripe.com/connect/charges]:
  - Destination charges: "You create a charge on your platform, so the payment appears in your platform's balance. A portion of the funds immediately transfers to the connected account's balance." Stripe recommends this for marketplaces where customers transact with the platform and there is one connected account per payment (e.g. "a branded service that uses independent contractors, such as a rideshare app"). This matches our model exactly: one client, one carer, one booking.
  - Separate charges and transfers: charge on the platform account, then a separate transfer; for multi-recipient or delayed-routing cases.
  - Direct charges: charge created on the connected account (carer would be merchant of record); the payment never appears in the platform balance at all.
- The nuance the solicitor should confirm: with destination charges the platform is the settlement merchant and the gross payment transits the platform's Stripe balance before the transfer. Stripe's analysis is that this balance is e-money/funds held by Stripe (the regulated EMI) in its safeguarding accounts, so the platform never "possesses or controls" funds in the PSRs sense; Stripe itself repeatedly says platforms should "obtain independent legal advice" on this [source: https://stripe.com/guides/frequently-asked-questions-about-stripe-connect-and-psd2]. There is also a fallback: the technical service provider exclusion, Schedule 1 Part 2 para 2(j), for "services provided by technical service providers, which support the provision of payment services, without the provider entering at any time into possession of the funds to be transferred" [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2017/752/schedule/1].
- What would break this: taking payments into our own company bank account (bank transfer "to help a client out"), handling cash, holding deposits ourselves, or refunding from company funds rather than through Stripe. Any of those puts us in possession of funds and back inside the perimeter with no commercial agent exclusion to fall back on.

## (c) Safeguarding and client money if we ever hold funds

- If we became an authorised payment institution we would have to safeguard "relevant funds" under regulation 23 PSRs 2017: either segregate them and, if held beyond the end of the business day following receipt, place them in a separate account with an authorised credit institution or the Bank of England (or secure liquid assets with an authorised custodian), or cover them with an insurance policy or comparable guarantee [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2017/752/regulation/23]. Small PIs may safeguard voluntarily [source: same].
- The regime is being tightened: FCA PS25/12 (August 2025) introduces interim "supplementary" safeguarding rules in a new CASS 15, in force 7 May 2026, including daily safeguarding reconciliations, resolution packs, monthly regulatory returns and an annual safeguarding audit (firms that have never safeguarded more than £100,000 over at least 53 weeks are exempt from the audit) [source: https://www.fca.org.uk/publication/policy/ps25-12.pdf; summary: https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/cda2937a/preparing-for-the-fcas-new-safeguarding-rules-a-countdown-to-7-may-2026]. CASS 15 supplements regulation 23 [source: https://www.fca.org.uk/publication/finalised-guidance/fca-approach-payment-services-electronic-money-2017.pdf, ch 1].
- Conclusion: holding client money ourselves would bring authorisation, capital, safeguarding accounts, daily reconciliation and audit obligations. For a pre-launch business this is a strong reason to design so that funds only ever sit with Stripe.

## (d) VAT on the commission

- The welfare exemption (VATA 1994 Sch 9 Group 7 [source: https://www.charitytaxgroup.org.uk/tax/vat/exemption/welfare/]) applies only to welfare services supplied by charities, public bodies, or "state regulated private welfare institutions or agencies", meaning providers "registered with, or regulated by" the CQC or equivalent bodies [source: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/welfare-services-and-goods-notice-7012]. We are deliberately not CQC-registered, so we cannot exempt anything under this heading.
- Agent vs principal is "determined by reference to your contractual and other arrangements", and HMRC's notice is explicit: "If you are an agent, your commission, fee or any other charge that you made for arranging and administering the supply is standard-rated" [source: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/welfare-services-and-goods-notice-7012].
- So on the intended structure (carer contracts directly with the client; platform is an introducer/agent): the platform charges VAT at 20% on its 15% commission and its 6% client fee only, once its own taxable turnover passes the £90,000 registration threshold [source: https://www.gov.uk/vat-registration/when-to-register]. The care fee itself is the carer's supply; a self-employed carer below £90,000 turnover is not VAT-registered so charges no VAT (and could not use the welfare exemption anyway, not being state-regulated) [source: https://www.gov.uk/vat-registration/when-to-register; https://www.gov.uk/guidance/welfare-services-and-goods-notice-7012].
- The bad case: if HMRC characterised the platform as principal (supplying care in its own name), the whole care fee would be standard-rated because the platform is not a charity or state-regulated [source: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/welfare-services-and-goods-notice-7012]. That would add 20% to gross prices AND would undermine the CQC introduction-agency position, since acting as principal implies ongoing direction and control. Contract drafting must keep the carer as the supplier of care and the platform as supplier of introduction/administration services to each side.
- Note the helpful alignment: for VAT we want to be an agent of both sides charging two fees; the PSRs commercial agent exclusion problem this creates (acting for both) is neutralised by never holding funds (Stripe).

## (e) Registration thresholds and other exclusions

- Small payment institution registration is available where average payment transactions turnover does not exceed EUR 3 million per month; cheaper and simpler than authorisation, no ongoing capital requirements, but conduct-of-business rules still apply [source: https://www.fca.org.uk/publication/finalised-guidance/fca-approach-payment-services-electronic-money-2017.pdf, paras 3.3-3.4]. This is the realistic fallback if we ever did fall inside the perimeter at our scale.
- Limited network exclusion (Sch 1 Pt 2 para 2(k)) covers payment instruments usable "only within a limited network of service providers which have direct commercial agreements with the issuer" [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2017/752/schedule/1]; users relying on it must notify the FCA under regulation 38 once transactions exceed EUR 1 million in any 12 months [source: https://www.fca.org.uk/publication/finalised-guidance/fca-approach-payment-services-electronic-money-2017.pdf, para 13.14]. Our analysis (not verified guidance): this does not fit us, because we do not issue a payment instrument; card payments via Stripe are general-purpose. Treat LNE as irrelevant unless the solicitor says otherwise.

## Market practice

- Stripe positions Connect precisely as the way European/UK marketplaces avoid needing their own payments licence after PSD2 narrowed the commercial agent exemption [source: https://stripe.com/guides/how-psd2-impacts-marketplaces-and-platforms].
- PrimeCarers, an introduction agency with no CQC registration, intermediates all payments: clients are billed weekly and "payments are paid into your stripe account, before then being transferred to your bank account" [source: https://primecarers.co.uk/faq/detail/how-are-payments-handled]; cash is banned except on-the-job expenses [source: https://primecarers.co.uk/faq/carer]. Its commissions (12.5% live-in, 15% visits of 6+ hours, 20% under 6 hours) are stated as inclusive of VAT in the carer terms [source: https://primecarers.co.uk/faq/carer; https://primecarers.co.uk/terms-of-service/carer], consistent with standard-rated agency commission.
- TrustonTap charges an 18% + VAT carer-side commission [source: https://www.trustontap.com/care-jobs/frequently-asked-questions/] and a 5% + VAT family-side administration fee [source: https://www.trustontap.com/care-services/our-pricing/]: VAT charged on the fees only, i.e. agent treatment, and a dual-sided fee structure like ours.
- HomeTouch quotes fees of "20% - 30% (incl. VAT) of stated carers rates" [source: https://myhometouch.com/terms-caremarketplace].

## Practical conclusion

With Stripe Connect destination charges, on Stripe's published analysis the platform never possesses or controls client funds, is not providing a payment service, and needs no FCA authorisation or registration at all; the regulated actor is Stripe Payments UK Ltd (EMI, FRN 900461). This is exactly how comparable UK care introduction platforms operate. The two things to get the solicitor to confirm are (1) that the gross amount transiting the platform's Stripe balance under destination charges does not count as possession or control, and (2) the contract drafting that keeps us agent (for VAT and CQC purposes) while funds stay with Stripe (for PSRs purposes).

## Questions to put to the solicitor

1. Under Stripe Connect destination charges the gross payment appears in our platform Stripe balance before the automatic transfer to the carer. Do you agree with Stripe's analysis that these funds are held by Stripe (the EMI) and are never in our "possession or control", so we provide no Schedule 1 Part 1 payment service and need no FCA authorisation or registration? Would direct charges or separate charges and transfers be materially safer?
2. Given we charge both sides (15% carer commission, 6% client fee) and contract with both, do you agree the commercial agent exclusion (Sch 1 Pt 2 para 2(b)) is unavailable to us, and does anything else turn on that while we never hold funds?
3. Are there flows in our design that could put us in possession of funds without noticing: refunds funded from our own account, goodwill credits, disputed-hours adjustments paid directly to carers, cancellation deposits, or clients paying by bank transfer when a card fails? What operational rules should we impose?
4. If we later want to hold funds (e.g. prepaid care packages or escrow), what is the realistic path and cost: small PI registration (under EUR 3m/month) versus authorised PI, including regulation 23 safeguarding and the CASS 15 rules in force from 7 May 2026?
5. On our draft terms, are we agent or principal for VAT? Please confirm VAT is chargeable at 20% on the 15% commission and the 6% client fee only, with no exposure to VAT on the gross care fee, and whether fees should be displayed VAT-inclusive to consumers.
6. Who should invoice the client for the care element: the carer directly, or the platform under a self-billing/agency billing arrangement, and what must the paperwork say to preserve both the VAT agency analysis and the CQC introduction-agency exemption?
7. Does Stripe's UK Connected Account Agreement or Services Agreement require specific wording in our client and carer terms (e.g. identifying Stripe as the payment services provider), and does anything in our terms risk contradicting the "platform never holds funds" position?
8. Is there any consumer credit angle if clients are billed weekly in arrears for care already delivered (deferred payment), or is that outside the Consumer Credit Act perimeter?

## Product mitigations if the answer is bad

- If destination charges are considered too close to holding funds: switch to direct charges on the carer's connected account (carer is merchant of record, payment never appears in the platform balance) with our 21-point take collected as a Stripe application fee; or use separate charges and transfers with on_behalf_of set to the carer.
- Hard operational rules regardless: no client payments into company bank accounts, no cash, all refunds and adjustments executed inside Stripe, no deposit/escrow features in v1.
- If we somehow still fall inside the perimeter: register as a small PI (our volumes will be far below EUR 3m/month at launch) rather than seek full authorisation, and safeguard voluntarily; or interpose a second regulated provider.
- If VAT principal risk is flagged: tighten the contract triangle (carer supplies care to client; platform supplies introduction and admin services separately to each side), adopt self-billing with clear agency wording, and show carer rate and platform fee as separate lines at checkout.
- Price presentation: display the 6% client fee VAT-inclusive from day one so a later VAT registration does not force a visible price rise.
