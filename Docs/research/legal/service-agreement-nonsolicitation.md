# The client-carer service agreement and non-solicitation / anti-circumvention clauses

Researched: 2026-07-08. This prepares questions for a solicitor. It is NOT legal advice.

Scope: England and Wales law, current position. The platform provides a template agreement between client and self-employed carer but is not a party to it; non-solicitation and circumvention-fee clauses sit in the platform-client and platform-carer terms.

## (a) What the client-carer service agreement should contain

Best publicly available benchmark: TrustonTap's Carer/Client Contract v8.0, a complete direct client-carer agreement [source: https://www.trustontap.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/TrustonTap-Carer-Client-Contract-8.0.pdf]. Its structure (verified from the document itself):

- **Formation**: contract + per-booking summary + any written/oral terms confirmed in writing form the agreement (cl 2.1, 2.4); minimum one-hour visits (definition of "Visit").
- **Carer duties** (cl 3.1): due care and skill, agreed times, safety compliance, PPE for personal care, two weeks' notice of holidays, 48 hours' notice if unable to attend, duty to report safeguarding concerns.
- **Client duties** (cl 3.2): safe fit-for-purpose premises, working equipment, funding in place, 48 hours' cancellation notice for visits (otherwise the carer may still charge), disclosure of challenging behaviours before booking.
- **Live-in extras** (cl 3.3): room and board, 2-hour daily break, food allowance.
- **Emergencies/substitution** (cl 3.4): carer uses reasonable endeavours to find a substitute; substitute contracts separately.
- **Payment** (cl 4): platform invoices on the carer's behalf from carer timesheets; mileage at approved rates; 50% "retention fee" during hospital/respite/holiday, capped at ~4 weeks (cl 4.5.2).
- **Termination** (cl 8): free walk-away after the introductory meeting; 14 days' notice for ongoing engagements (48 hours in week one); payment in lieu of notice; death of care recipient treated as notice given.
- **Status** (cl 9): independent contractor; carer indemnifies client for tax/NI and employment-status claims (cl 9.2); both parties acknowledge the platform is a CQC "introductory agency" with no ongoing direction or control of care (cl 9.3-9.4); enhanced DBS required for personal care (cl 3.7).
- Confidentiality, UK GDPR data handling, mediation via the platform, severability, England and Wales law.

Notably absent from the TrustonTap contract: an express requirement that the carer hold public liability insurance. Our template should add one (Curam treats attempting to "circumvent platform fees or insurance" as grounds for immediate termination of the account [source: https://www.curamcare.com/tscs]).

Consumer-law overlay on the template:
- Unfair-terms control applies to the carer-client contract too (carer is a trader, client a consumer): unfair terms are not binding on consumers [source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/unfair-contract-terms-cma37/unfair-contracts-what-do-businesses-need-to-know-short-guide]. The CRA 2015 test is whether, contrary to good faith, a term causes significant imbalance to the consumer's detriment [source: https://www.addleshawgoddard.com/globalassets/specialisms/financial-regulation/consumer-rights-act-guidance.pdf].
- The Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013 give consumers a 14-day cancellation right for distance contracts for services, plus pre-contract information duties [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/3134]. Whether an individual carer booking via our platform is making a "distance contract" under an organised scheme, and who owes the information duties, is UNCERTAIN: a solicitor question.
- TrustonTap-style charges (retention fee, charging for the whole of a short-term booking on early cancellation) sit close to CRA Schedule 2 grey-list territory (see (c)) and need a fairness check before we copy them.

## (b) Enforceability: carer side (restraint of trade) and fee clauses (penalty doctrine)

**Restraint of trade (carer-side non-solicitation).** Covenants in restraint of trade are prima facie unenforceable and enforceable only if reasonable by reference to the parties' interests and the public interest. In East England Schools CIC v Palmer [2013] EWHC 4138 (QB), a 6-month non-solicitation/non-dealing covenant protecting a recruitment agency's connections with clients and candidates was upheld: 6 months was what the agency needed to re-secure the goodwill of its clients and candidates after the consultant left [source: https://employmentcasesupdate.co.uk/content/east-england-schools-cic-v-palmer-anor-2013-ewhc-4138-qb.52876503a5d34a76ba637fc2cc46052c.htm]. A recruitment/introduction platform's interest in its client-carer connections is closely analogous. That case concerned an employee; how the doctrine applies to a platform-carer contract of services (arguably less inequality of bargaining power than employment, but still an individual vs a business) is UNCERTAIN in this exact context. Design rules that follow from the doctrine: restrict only clients actually introduced to that carer, keep the duration short, and offer a buy-out route rather than a blanket ban.

**Penalty doctrine (fixed circumvention fees).** Cavendish Square Holding BV v Makdessi; ParkingEye Ltd v Beavis [2015] UKSC 67 replaced "genuine pre-estimate of loss" with this test: is the clause "a secondary obligation which imposes a detriment on the contract-breaker out of all proportion to any legitimate interest of the innocent party" in enforcing the primary obligation [source: https://www.aoshearman.com/en/insights/newpenaltytest]. Legitimate interests extend beyond compensation (deterrence, protecting a business system or goodwill) [source: https://www.hsfkramer.com/insights/2016-02/landmark-decision-of-english-supreme-court-on-penalty-clauses-and-enforceability-of]. In ParkingEye the £85 charge, far above any loss, was upheld because the operator had a legitimate interest in making the parking scheme work [source: https://www.aoshearman.com/en/insights/newpenaltytest]. The rule bites only on secondary obligations triggered by breach; a fee framed as a primary obligation (a price for a permitted choice, e.g. a buy-out fee for hiring the carer directly) may fall outside the doctrine altogether, though courts look at substance and the primary/secondary line remains contested [source: https://www.aoshearman.com/en/insights/newpenaltytest].

Practical read-across: a circumvention fee pitched as recovery of lost commission (15% carer-side + 6% client-side of the expected remaining booking value) serves an obvious legitimate interest and, if the quantum is reconcilable to that interest and steps down over time, is well placed under Makdessi. PrimeCarers still recites the pre-2015 "bona fide pre-estimate of loss" language for its £5,000 liquidated damages [source: https://primecarers.co.uk/terms-of-service/client]; the modern drafting anchor is legitimate interest plus proportionality.

## (c) Consumer-side enforceability: CRA 2015 and CMA guidance

Against a consumer client the fee must survive two doctrines at once: the penalty rule and the CRA 2015 fairness test. The CRA Schedule 2 grey list expressly captures both flavours of circumvention fee [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2015/15/schedule/2]:
- Para 5: requiring a consumer who decides not to conclude or perform the contract to pay "a disproportionately high sum in compensation or for services which have not been supplied".
- Para 6: requiring a consumer who fails to fulfil his obligations "to pay a disproportionately high sum in compensation".

CMA guidance (CMA37) tells businesses to take particular care with terms imposing financial sanctions such as cancellation fees, to use plain language, and not to hide important wording: terms that surprise the consumer, or are buried in small print, are at risk; unfair terms are simply not binding [source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/unfair-contract-terms-cma37/unfair-contracts-what-do-businesses-need-to-know-short-guide]. The CMA consulted on revised unfair-terms guidance (consultation closed 19 March 2026); watch for the final version [source: https://www.rpclegal.com/snapshots/consumer/spring-2026/cma-publishes-draft-revised-guidance-on-unfair-contract-terms-under-the-consumer-rights-act/].

ParkingEye is also the leading consumer-fairness authority: the majority (6-1) held the £85 charge was not unfair under the (predecessor) Unfair Terms in Consumer Contracts Regulations 1999 because, despite a significant imbalance, there was no breach of good faith: the term was prominent, simple, and a reasonable consumer in the motorist's position would have agreed to it; Lord Toulson dissented that the charge exceeded any damages and ignored individual circumstances [source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavendish_Square_Holding_BV_v_Talal_El_Makdessi]. Translation for us: prominence and comprehensibility at sign-up are as important as quantum. A four-figure fee buried in clause 10 of unread T&Cs is far more vulnerable than the same fee flagged in plain English at booking.

**A possible statutory landmine.** For employment businesses, the Conduct of Employment Agencies and Employment Businesses Regulations 2003, reg 10, makes temp-to-perm "transfer fees" unenforceable unless the hirer is offered an extended-hire alternative, and only allows them where the transfer happens within 14 weeks of the first supply or 8 weeks of the end of an assignment [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2003/3319/regulation/10; https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/conduct-regulations-2003-guidance-for-employment-agencies-and-employment-businesses/overview-of-the-conduct-regulations-2003]. Whether an introduction platform for self-employed carers is an "employment agency" or "employment business" under the Employment Agencies Act 1973 at all is UNCERTAIN and is a threshold solicitor question: if the regime applies, much of the market's circumvention-fee practice (and carer-side commission) would need re-examination.

## (e) Duration: what would a court likely uphold?

- Market range: 12 months from introduction (PrimeCarers, HomeTouch) up to 24 months post-termination (TrustonTap). No reported case tests these platforms' clauses; the 24-month tail looks aggressive against the case law below.
- Closest authority: 6 months was upheld as reasonable to protect a recruitment agency's client and candidate connections in East England Schools v Palmer [source: https://employmentcasesupdate.co.uk/content/east-england-schools-cic-v-palmer-anor-2013-ewhc-4138-qb.52876503a5d34a76ba637fc2cc46052c.htm]. Restraints must go no further than necessary; longer periods need justification (e.g. length of typical care relationships and replacement cycles).
- If the Conduct Regulations were held to apply, the statutory comparator is dramatically shorter: transfer fees only within 14 weeks of first supply / 8 weeks of the end of an assignment, with an extended-hire option [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2003/3319/regulation/10].
- Best defensible design for a lost-commission fee: 6-12 months from the last paid booking (not open-ended from introduction), quantum derived from actual booking history (e.g. 21% total take on the trailing average weekly hours, projected over a capped period), stepping down with tenure, with a fixed modest buy-out as a permitted primary-obligation alternative (the Curam £750-after-90-days pattern). This is analysis, not settled law: confirm with the solicitor.

## Questions to put to the solicitor

1. Threshold: is the platform an "employment agency" or "employment business" under the Employment Agencies Act 1973 / Conduct Regulations 2003 when introducing self-employed carers to private clients? If yes, do reg 10 transfer-fee limits apply to our client-side circumvention fee, and does the prohibition on charging work-seekers affect our 15% carer-side commission?
2. Should the circumvention fee be drafted as a primary obligation (a buy-out price for a permitted choice) or as liquidated damages for breach? Which structure better survives Makdessi and CRA 2015 scrutiny for a consumer client?
3. What quantum would you defend: a fixed fee (market £1,000-£5,000) or a formula capped at demonstrable lost commission (21% of projected remaining booking value)? Is a stepped-down schedule materially safer?
4. For the carer-side clause: is 12 months from the last paid booking with that client defensible as a restraint of trade, and should it be limited to clients the carer actually served (not merely was shown)?
5. Do the Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013 apply to the client-carer contract formed via the platform (distance contract; carer as trader)? Who must give the pre-contract information and 14-day cancellation notice, and what happens to fees if it is not given?
6. We are not a party to the client-carer agreement. Should our enforcement rights live only in our own platform terms, or should the template also give us third-party rights under the Contracts (Rights of Third Parties) Act 1999?
7. Are TrustonTap-style template charges (50% retention fee during hospital stays, charging out the full remainder of a cancelled short-term booking) fair under CRA Schedule 2 paras 5-7 for consumer clients, or should we soften them?
8. How much does presentation matter in practice: does a separate plain-English acknowledgment of the circumvention fee at booking (per ParkingEye's prominence reasoning) materially strengthen enforceability?
9. Any consumer-enforcement risk (CMA/Trading Standards) in publishing a deterrent figure higher than we would actually pursue, as PrimeCarers appears to do with the discretionary £2,500/£5,000 structure?

## Product mitigations if the answer is bad

- **Fee restructure**: replace any fixed four-figure fee with a formula capped at actual lost commission over a defined period, computed from real booking history; show the current buy-out figure in the client's dashboard so it is transparent and never surprising.
- **Buy-out as a feature**: after 90 days of platform billing, offer a published, modest "hire your carer" fee (Curam pattern). A priced option is both commercially honest and structurally a primary obligation.
- **Shorter tail**: drop to 6 months from the last paid booking; avoid TrustonTap's 24-month post-termination reach.
- **Retention through value, not deterrence**: bundle what off-platform hiring loses the client (payment handling, insurance verification, cover carers, DBS/reference checks, records for CQC-exemption evidence) so circumvention is unattractive regardless of the clause.
- **Prominence engineering**: surface the fee in plain English at booking confirmation with a separate acknowledgment; keep the clause out of small print.
- **If the Conduct Regulations apply**: add an extended-hire alternative to any transfer fee, confine the fee to the statutory windows, and take the fee exclusively from the client side; re-verify the carer commission model.
