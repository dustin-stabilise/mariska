# Platform terms of business: structure and required content for clients and professionals

Researched: 2026-07-08, prepares solicitor questions, NOT legal advice.

## Contract architecture (the starting decision)

An introduction platform typically runs three contracts: (1) platform–client terms, (2) platform–carer terms, (3) a direct client–carer care agreement that the platform facilitates but is not party to. Curam's live terms state exactly this: "Curam introduces carers and clients. A Service Agreement forms a contract directly between the client and the carer" [source: https://www.curamcare.com/tscs]. This split matters legally: the carer is the trader supplying the care service, the platform is the trader supplying the introduction/booking/payment service, and each owes its own consumer-law duties to the client for its own service.

## Client-side: Consumer Rights Act 2015, services chapter (Part 1 Ch 4, ss 48–57)

- s49: every service contract includes a term that the service is performed with reasonable care and skill [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2015/15/part/1/chapter/4].
- s50: statements the trader makes about itself or the service (marketing copy, profile claims) become binding contractual terms if the consumer takes them into account [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2015/15/part/1/chapter/4]. Platform copy about vetting, DBS checks and carer quality is therefore contractually load-bearing.
- s51 reasonable price where none fixed; s52 reasonable time; ss54–56 remedies (repeat performance, price reduction up to full refund, paid within 14 days); s57: liability for s49 cannot be excluded or restricted [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2015/15/part/1/chapter/4].

## Client-side: Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013 (SI 2013/3134), distance contracts

Both the platform contract and each booking concluded online are distance contracts. Duties:

- Reg 13 + Schedule 2: pre-contract information including main characteristics; trader identity, geographical address and contact details; complaint-handling address and policy; the total price inclusive of taxes or how it will be calculated; payment and performance arrangements; conditions, time limit and procedures for cancelling plus the model cancellation form; that the consumer must pay reasonable costs if cancelling after requesting early performance; when the cancellation right is lost; contract duration and renewal terms; out-of-court dispute mechanisms [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/3134/schedule/2].
- Reg 14: electronic ordering requirements (order button must make the payment obligation clear); reg 16: confirmation of the contract on a durable medium [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/3134/contents].
- Regs 29–30: 14-day cancellation right for service contracts, running from conclusion of the contract; reg 31: the period is extended where the cancellation information was not given [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/3134/contents].
- Reg 36, the critical one for care starting quickly: the consumer must make an express request for the service to begin within the 14 days; if the consumer then cancels, they pay a proportionate amount for the service supplied up to notification; the consumer pays nothing if the trader failed to give the required cancellation and cost information; and under reg 36(2) the cancellation right is lost once the service is fully performed, provided performance began on the consumer's express request and the consumer acknowledged they would lose the right on completion [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/3134/regulation/36]. Checkout must therefore capture (a) an express request to start early and (b) the acknowledgment, per booking.
- Reg 40: no additional payments without express consent obtained before the consumer is bound; consent cannot be inferred from pre-ticked boxes or default options; wrongly taken payments must be reimbursed [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/3134/regulation/40]. The 6% client fee must be actively presented, never a default add-on.

## Client-side: unfair terms (CRA 2015 Part 2)

- s62: a term is unfair if, contrary to good faith, it causes a significant imbalance in the parties' rights and obligations to the consumer's detriment; unfair terms do not bind the consumer [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2015/15/part/2].
- s63 + Schedule 2: "grey list" of potentially unfair terms (e.g. disproportionate cancellation charges, unilateral variation, limiting liability) [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2015/15/part/2].
- s64: price and main-subject-matter terms escape the fairness test only if transparent AND prominent; s65: liability for death or personal injury from negligence can never be excluded; s68: plain and intelligible language; s69: ambiguity read in the consumer's favour; s71: courts must consider fairness of their own motion [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2015/15/part/2].
- Highest-risk clauses in a platform like ours: liability caps, late-cancellation charges, off-platform "recruitment fee" clauses (penalty-flavoured), unilateral fee changes, and auto-renewing subscriptions.

## Client-side: fee transparency (DMCC Act 2024)

- s230 DMCCA: an invitation to purchase must state the total price including "any fees, taxes, charges or other payments that the consumer will necessarily incur", or, where it cannot reasonably be calculated in advance, how it will be calculated, presented with equal prominence [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2024/13/section/230]. The 6% client-side fee is a mandatory charge: hourly prices shown to clients must be the fee-inclusive total (or the fee formula shown with equal prominence).
- The CMA published final price-transparency guidance (CMA209) in November 2025 and simultaneously opened its first DMCCA investigations into drip pricing, mandatory-fee presentation and default opt-ins [source: https://www.osborneclarke.com/insights/uk-cma-finalises-guidance-price-transparency-under-dmcca-and-begins-enforcement-action] [guidance PDF: https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/691b10065a253e2c40d705d9/Price_transparency_-_CMA209_.pdf].

## Professional-side terms: status, substitution, mutuality, and their limits

- Terms should state: carer is a self-employed independent contractor; sets own rates; no obligation to accept work; may (genuinely) send a substitute; responsible for own tax, insurance and training; contracts directly with the client. Curam's terms do this ("Carers operate as independent contractors", "never any obligation to accept work") [source: https://www.curamcare.com/tscs].
- Limit: written labels do not decide status. In Uber BV v Aslam [2021] UKSC 5 the Supreme Court held worker status is determined by the reality of the relationship, not the contractual wording, applying Autoclenz v Belcher: terms misrepresenting the true relationship do not dictate the outcome [source: https://www.supremecourt.uk/cases/uksc-2019-0029]. Status wording only holds if the product genuinely avoids control (no rate-setting by platform, no penalties for refusing work, no performance management of care delivery).
- These are B2B-ish contracts, so CRA 2015 Part 2 does not protect the carer; UCTA 1977 reasonableness control on standard written terms may still apply UNCERTAIN (not verified against a fetched source).

## Conduct of Employment Agencies and Employment Businesses Regulations 2003 (SI 2003/3319), IF in scope

Scope trigger: Employment Agencies Act 1973 s13 defines an "employment agency" as a business "of providing services... for the purpose of finding persons employment with employers or of supplying employers with persons for employment", and "employment" expressly includes "employment by way of a professional engagement or otherwise under a contract for services" [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1973/35/section/13]. Introducing self-employed carers to paying clients is plausibly caught. Whether it is in fact caught (and agency vs employment business) is a separate research question; if caught:

- Reg 16 (agencies): agreement to terms must be obtained before first providing work-finding services; terms must state the services provided, authority to act, whether the agency may hold money for the work-seeker, full fee details (amount or calculation method, refund/rebate circumstances), and termination notice; no variation "unless the work-seeker agrees", with the varied terms recorded and delivered by the end of the fifth business day; continued services cannot be made conditional on accepting a variation [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2003/3319/regulation/16].
- Regs 14–15 (employment businesses) are the parallel duties: terms agreed before services, recorded in a single document, stating the type of work and reg 15 content; same no-variation-without-agreement and 5-business-day record rules [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2003/3319/regulation/14].
- Reg 22 imposes additional requirements where work-seekers will work with vulnerable persons; reg 13A requires a key information document (employment businesses) [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2003/3319/contents].
- Fee risk: EAA 1973 s6 prohibits an agency from requesting or receiving "any fee from any person" for finding them employment, except in cases prescribed by regulations (reg 26 prescribes narrow classes, mainly entertainment/modelling); breach is a criminal offence [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1973/35/section/6] [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2003/3319/contents]. If the Conduct regime applies, the 15% carer-side commission is directly exposed. Market practice (Curam, PrimeCarers) charges carer-side commission at scale, so the sector evidently takes the view it is out of scope or structured around this, but that is market practice, not law.

## Provision of Services Regulations 2009 (SI 2009/2999)

- Reg 8: the platform must make available its name, legal status and form, geographic address and contact details, registration details, VAT number, any authorisation scheme, general terms and conditions, any terms on applicable law/jurisdiction, after-sales guarantees, the price where pre-determined, main features of the service, and professional liability insurance details; "made available" includes easy electronic accessibility at an address supplied by the provider (a website legal page suffices) [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2009/2999/regulation/8].
- Reg 7 (contact details), reg 10 (dispute-resolution information) and reg 12 (complaints) also apply [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2009/2999/contents]. Self-employed carers are service providers with the same duties for their own service; the platform can discharge much of this for them via profile fields.

## Data protection clauses (UK GDPR roles)

- Controller: whoever "alone or jointly with others, determines the purposes and means of the processing"; processor: processes "on behalf of the controller" (Art 4(7)–(8)) [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/eur/2016/679/article/4]. ICO guidance: status is determined by the facts, not by contract labels, and a sole trader or self-employed professional can be a controller in their own right [source: https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/controllers-and-processors/controllers-and-processors/what-are-controllers-and-processors/].
- Likely mapping: platform = controller for account, matching, payments and platform-safety data; carer = independent controller for care records they create (they decide purposes of their own business records); client = arguably outside UK GDPR when arranging their own family's care, via the "purely personal or household activity" exemption (Art 2(2)(a)) [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/eur/2016/679/article/2]. Joint controllership (Art 26) is a risk wherever the platform and carer jointly determine purposes (e.g. shared care notes features); needs solicitor confirmation.
- Terms should therefore contain: a role-allocation clause (each party an independent controller for its own processing, with a processor annex only if the platform hosts care records on carers' behalf), a carer obligation to comply with UK GDPR including special category (health) data conditions, confidentiality obligations, breach-notification cooperation, and a link to the privacy notice. Care data is health data, so lawful-basis and Art 9 condition wording must be checked by the solicitor.

## Market practice: Curam's published terms (section map)

Single unified T&Cs document with client/carer-specific provisions inline, sections in order: About Curam; Our Services; Care Service Models; User Accounts and Conduct; Carer Status; Payments, Fees and Funding; VAT; Insurance; Cancellations, Refunds and Shadow Shifts; Off-Platform Engagement and Recruitment Fees; Monitoring, AI and Automated Systems; Liability; Complaints; Data Protection; Intellectual Property; General; Governing Law [source: https://www.curamcare.com/tscs]. Notable content: direct client–carer Service Agreement; carers as independent contractors with no obligation to accept work; insurance valid only when the shift is invoiced and paid through the platform; off-platform payments prohibited, with a £750 + VAT recruitment fee after 90 days (£1,000 + VAT without approval); liability capped at the greater of 12 months' fees or £5,000, excluding death/personal injury from negligence; disputes to be resolved directly between client and carer [source: https://www.curamcare.com/tscs]. The off-platform fee and the insurance-conditionality clauses are the ones most likely to attract CRA Part 2 fairness scrutiny if replicated.

## Questions to put to the solicitor

1. Should we run one unified T&Cs document (Curam style) or three documents (client terms, carer terms, template client–carer care agreement), and who should be the counterparty to the care agreement?
2. For each booking, is the distance contract with the 14-day cancellation right the client–carer care agreement, the platform contract, or both, and who bears the reg 13/Sch 2 information duties for each?
3. Is our checkout flow (express request to start within 14 days + acknowledgment of losing the right on full performance, captured per booking) sufficient under reg 36, and how should "fully performed" be read for recurring weekly care: per visit, per booking block, or per contract?
4. If the client cancels mid-booking under reg 36, how must the "proportionate amount" be calculated and displayed, and can our late-cancellation charge (à la Curam's 48-hour rule) coexist with it without being an unfair term?
5. Are we an "employment agency" under EAA 1973 s13 on this model, and if so does the s6/reg 26 fee prohibition catch a 15% commission deducted from the carer's hourly rate via Stripe Connect? If yes, what restructuring (client-side-only fee, carer subscription, or other) is lawful?
6. If the Conduct Regulations apply, produce reg 16-compliant carer terms (or reg 14/15 if we are an employment business) including the no-variation mechanics; if they do not apply, confirm what variation mechanism for carer terms is safe.
7. Does displaying carer hourly rates without the 6% client fee breach DMCCA s230, and exactly how must the fee appear in search results, profiles and checkout under CMA209?
8. Review our liability clause: what cap is defensible against CRA s57/s65 and Part 2 fairness, given we are introduction-only and vetting claims are s50-binding?
9. Is an off-platform circumvention fee (fixed sum after N days) enforceable against clients (CRA Part 2 / penalty doctrine) and against carers, and what drafting maximises survival?
10. Confirm the UK GDPR role allocation (platform controller, carer independent controller, client household-exempt), whether any joint controllership arises from shared care-notes features, and whether we need an Art 26 arrangement or processor terms.
11. Do carers count as consumers for any of our platform terms (e.g. new carers signing up before trading), and does UCTA 1977 s3 apply to our standard carer terms?
12. What must our terms say about holding client money (Stripe Connect flow) if reg 16(a agencies) "money on behalf of the work-seeker" wording is engaged, and do we need client-money handling provisions regardless?

## Product mitigations if the answer is bad

- Conduct Regs catch the carer commission: flip fees fully client-side (e.g. 0% carer / ~21% client) or move carers to a subscription/SaaS fee; keep Stripe Connect but re-paper who pays what.
- Reg 36 makes early-start refunds painful: build per-booking express-request + acknowledgment checkboxes, pro-rata refund automation, and delay non-urgent starts past day 14 by default.
- DMCCA pricing: show fee-inclusive hourly totals everywhere a price appears (search cards, profiles, checkout); keep the carer's own rate visible but secondary.
- Liability cap challenged: carry platform insurance sized to a higher cap, and never cap death/personal-injury negligence liability.
- Off-platform fee unenforceable: rely on product mechanics instead (insurance and dispute support valid only for on-platform bookings, delayed contact-detail reveal) rather than the fee clause.
- Joint controllership found: split the product so care notes live in a carer-controlled space with the platform as processor under a DPA annex, or drop shared care-notes features at v1.
- Conduct Regs terms mechanics apply: version every carer T&Cs change, require click-through re-acceptance, never gate continued access on accepting a variation, and store the dated agreement record.
