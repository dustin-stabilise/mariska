# Employment Agencies Act 1973 and the 15% carer-side commission

Researched: 2026-07-08. This document prepares questions for a solicitor. It is NOT legal advice.

## Short answer (risk summary)

On the plain words of the statute, the current authority, and current GOV.UK guidance, a platform that introduces self-employed carers to private families is an **employment agency** under the Employment Agencies Act 1973 (EAA), and a 15% commission deducted from carer earnings is at real risk of being a prohibited work-finding fee under s.6(1). Care is not an exempt occupation. Yet all three named comparators (Curam, PrimeCarers, TrustonTap) openly charge carer-side commission and there is no known enforcement against them. The gap between the black-letter position and universal market practice is the central issue to put to the solicitor.

## (a) Definitions: employment agency vs employment business, and scope

- s.13(2) EAA: an "employment agency" is "the business of providing services for finding persons employment with employers or supplying employers with persons for employment", whether or not for profit [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1973/35/section/13].
- s.13(1): "employment" is defined widely to include "(a) employment by way of a professional engagement or otherwise under a contract for services", and "worker" and "employer" are "construed accordingly". So a family engaging a carer under a contract for services is an "employer" and the carer a "worker" for EAA purposes, whatever their status for tax or employment-rights purposes [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1973/35/section/13].
- s.13(3), as amended with effect from January 2026 (Employment Rights Act 2025 umbrella-company changes), redefines "employment business" as "participating in employment arrangements", where "participating" includes being the employer of supplied persons, supplying them, or "paying for or receiving payment for their services" [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1973/35/section/13]. Our model (no employment of carers, no control after introduction) points to employment agency, not employment business, but the new "receiving payment for their services" limb is untested against payment-handling marketplaces and should be raised with the solicitor.
- GOV.UK guidance (last updated 22 June 2026) states the regime covers "online platforms" among agency types, and that the Act "applies irrespective of the employment status of the work-seeker" [source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/conduct-regulations-2003-guidance-for-employment-agencies-and-employment-businesses/overview-of-the-conduct-regulations-2003].
- CQC treats care introduction agencies as outside CQC registration where they have no "ongoing role" in the direction or control of the care [source: https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-providers/registration/personal-care-ongoing-role-introductory-agencies-individual-care]. Note: the CQC page itself does not mention the EAS or the Conduct Regulations; the point that introductory agencies fall under the EAS remit as "employment agencies" (and managed agencies as employment businesses) is made by the Low Incomes Tax Reform Group [source: https://www.litrg.org.uk/employers/independent-living-paying-care/using-agency-find-carer].
- s.13(7) as enacted excluded certain services (ex-forces resettlement, nurse supply agencies as then defined, local authority functions, university appointment boards, etc.) [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1973/35/section/13/enacted]. The nurses-agency exclusion was repealed by the Care Standards Act 2000 with effect from 1 April 2003 (England) / 2 October 2003 (Wales); the current s.13(7) exclusions (military/ex-forces services, childminder agencies, various public authority functions) do not assist a care platform [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1973/35/section/13].

## (b) The s.6 fee prohibition and its exemptions

- s.6(1) EAA: except in prescribed cases, "a person carrying on an employment agency shall not request or directly or indirectly receive any fee from any person for providing services ... for the purpose of finding him employment" [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1973/35/section/6]. Breach is a criminal offence, punishable by a fine not exceeding the statutory maximum on summary conviction, or an unlimited fine on conviction on indictment [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1973/35/section/6].
- s.13(1): "fee" "includes any charge however described", so labelling the 15% as "commission", "service fee" or "platform fee" does not by itself take it outside s.6 [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1973/35/section/13].
- The prescribed exemptions are in reg 26 of the Conduct of Employment Agencies and Employment Businesses Regulations 2003 (SI 2003/3319): the s.6(1)(a) restriction "shall not apply in respect of a fee charged by an agency for ... finding or seeking to find a work-seeker employment in any of the occupations listed in Schedule 3", and even then only as "a charge or commission payable out of the work-seeker's earnings" [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2003/3319/regulation/26].
- Schedule 3 lists only: performers (actor, musician, singer, dancer, background artist etc.), specified creative/production roles, photographic or fashion models, and professional sports persons [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2003/3319/schedule/3]. **Care work is not listed. There is no exemption a care platform can use.**
- Even within Schedule 3, the exception is lost "where the agency, or any person connected with it, charges a fee to the hirer in respect of the service of supplying or introducing that work-seeker" [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2003/3319/regulation/26]. The statute's structure therefore disfavours charging both sides for the same introduction; our 15% + 6% dual-fee model charges both sides.
- Charging the hirer is unregulated: GOV.UK's Conduct Regulations guidance states fee levels charged to hirers are treated as "a commercial business to business agreement" and are not regulated by the Act or the Regulations (transfer fees aside) [source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/conduct-regulations-2003-guidance-for-employment-agencies-and-employment-businesses/overview-of-the-conduct-regulations-2003]. (The FWA complaint page previously cited here does not contain this statement.)
- Additional (non-work-finding) services may be charged for, but access to work-finding services must not be made conditional on buying them [source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/conduct-regulations-2003-guidance-for-employment-agencies-and-employment-businesses/overview-of-the-conduct-regulations-2003].

## (c) Is a self-employed carer contracting with a family a "work-seeker"?

- Reg 2 of the 2003 Regulations: a "work-seeker" is "a person to whom an agency or employment business provides or holds itself out as being capable of providing work-finding services"; a "hirer" is anyone to whom the agency introduces or supplies a work-seeker [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2003/3319/regulation/2].
- Because s.13(1) "employment" includes contracts for services, a self-employed carer using the platform to obtain engagements with families is a work-seeker, and the family is a hirer/employer, on the face of the legislation [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1973/35/section/13].
- This was tested and confirmed in Simply Learning (below). The main counter-argument (the carer is a business seeking customers, not a person seeking employment) is essentially the argument that lost in that case.

## (d) Case law and enforcement posture

**Simply Learning Tutor Agency Ltd & Ors v Secretary of State for BEIS [2020] EWHC 2461 (Admin), 18 September 2020.** Nine tutor-introduction businesses (structurally almost identical to a care introduction platform: self-employed tutors contract directly with parents) sought a declaration that they were outside the EAA. Helen Mountfield QC, sitting as a deputy High Court judge, refused and declared the opposite: "the definition of 'employment' in section 13(1) of the EAA includes ... persons, whether natural or legal, including tutors, who provide their services on a self-employed basis as an independent contractor, and the meanings assigned to 'employment agency' and 'employment business' ... must be construed accordingly" (para 61) [source: http://www.bailii.org/ew/cases/EWHC/Admin/2020/2461.html]. The case arose out of BEIS/EAS investigations into the platforms (including Tutorfair) from 2017 onwards. Law-firm commentary (Mishcon de Reya, 8 February 2021) reads it as meaning introduction platforms cannot evade the regime by claiming a passive hosting role ("simply providing information on a website or app puts a business into the realms of this regime") or by structuring arrangements as self-employed contracts [source: https://www.mishcon.com/news/confirmation-from-the-high-court-that-independent-contractors-are-covered-by-the-recruitment-industry-regulatory-regime]. The judgment decided scope, not s.6 directly; there is no reported decision applying s.6 to a percentage commission on a marketplace.

**Enforcement.** The Employment Agency Standards Inspectorate (EAS) was replaced by the Fair Work Agency (FWA), which began operating on 7 April 2026 under the Employment Rights Act 2025 [source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/making-a-complaint-to-the-employment-agency-standards-inspectorate-eas-about-your-recruitment-agency/making-a-complaint-to-the-employment-agency-standards-inspectorate-eas-about-your-recruitment-agency]. The FWA enforcement statement (19 May 2026) sets out a graduated, compliance-first approach: warning letters, labour market enforcement undertakings and orders, prohibition orders of up to 10 years under s.3A EAA (expressly including "a person from running online job boards that were caught by section 13" as employment agency or employment business activity), and criminal prosecution reserved for serious, deliberate or persistent non-compliance [source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/fair-work-agency-enforcement-policy-statement/fair-work-agency-enforcement-statement]. No published EAS/FWA guidance, prosecution or enforcement action specific to care introduction platforms or their commission models was found. One tutoring-sector commentary claims EAS post-2020 expected tutors to be paid directly or via split payments with agencies invoicing only their own commission, but it cites no official source: treat as UNCERTAIN [source: https://www.wise.live/blog/ewhc-2461-high-court-ruling-uk-tutoring-agencies/].

**Consultations.** Government consulted on payment handling by agencies in the entertainment, sport and modelling sectors (the Sch 3 world) [source: https://www.gov.uk/government/consultations/payment-handling-by-employment-agencies-in-the-entertainment-sport-and-modelling-sectors/outcome/consultation-response-on-payment-handling-by-employment-agencies-in-the-entertainment-sport-and-modelling-sectors]. No consultation specifically on supply-side charging by care or gig platforms was found.

## Market practice: how Curam, PrimeCarers and TrustonTap frame carer-side fees

- **Curam**: 15% commission on all shift invoices, deducted from the carer's set rate (site example: a £19.74/hr rate yields £16.77/hr to the carer); the current page presents a flat 15% and does not break out a 12.5% + VAT decomposition [source: https://www.curamcare.com/carer-information]. Its T&Cs frame Curam as a platform/introduction facilitator, "not a party" to the client-carer agreement, with no mention of the EAA or Conduct Regulations; a £1,000 + VAT off-platform introduction fee is charged to clients [source: https://www.curamcare.com/tscs].
- **PrimeCarers**: commission of 12.5% (live-in), 15% (visits 6h+), 20% (visits under 6h), "taken from the amount paid to the carer" [source: https://primecarers.co.uk/faq/carer]. Carer terms call it a "service fee", state PrimeCarers is "classified as an 'introductory agency'" and does not "act as an employment or care agency"; clients pay PrimeCarers, which deducts the fee before paying the carer. No EAA reference [source: https://primecarers.co.uk/terms-of-service/carer].
- **TrustonTap**: 18% + VAT commission on the carer's rate (£25/hr yields £19.60 to the carer) [source: https://www.trustontap.com/care-jobs/frequently-asked-questions/] plus a client-side 5% + VAT administration fee, i.e. dual-sided like our model [source: https://www.trustontap.com/care-services/our-pricing/].
- Structural observations: all three deduct from carer earnings rather than charging the client the full margin; all self-describe as "introduction/introductory agency" or "platform" and none engages publicly with the EAA fee prohibition. None appears to rely on a client-pays framing that would clearly take the fee outside s.6. Their apparent safety may rest on non-enforcement rather than a legal structure.

## What is genuinely unresolved

1. Whether a percentage commission deducted from a carer's earnings is a fee "for providing services ... for the purpose of finding him employment" (caught), or can be characterised as payment for ongoing platform services (payments, invoicing, insurance, profile hosting) distinct from work-finding (arguably not caught). No authority either way; s.13(1)'s "any charge however described" and s.6's "directly or indirectly receive" cut against the re-characterisation.
2. Why the incumbents' models have not been enforced against since Simply Learning: unknown; the FWA's complaint-led, compliance-first posture may explain it, but that is not a safe harbour, and FWA is newer and better resourced than EAS.
3. Whether the January 2026 "employment business" definition (participating includes "receiving payment for their services") pulls payment-handling marketplaces into the employment business regime, with its heavier obligations. Untested.
4. Whether structuring the entire margin as a client-side fee (carer receives 100% of their set rate) fully cures the issue where the platform still handles the money flow. Very likely yes on fees, but the payment-handling point in 3 remains.

## Questions to put to the solicitor

1. On the model as designed (carer sets rate, platform deducts 15% from carer, adds 6% to client), is the 15% a fee received from a work-seeker contrary to s.6(1)(a) EAA 1973, given Simply Learning and the absence of a Schedule 3 exemption?
2. Can the same economics be achieved lawfully by charging the client a single fee of about 21% on top of the carer's set rate, with the carer contractually entitled to 100% of their rate? Is there any anti-avoidance risk in s.6's "directly or indirectly" wording if the client-side fee is calculated off the carer's rate?
3. If some carer-side charge is retained, can it be defended as a charge for non-work-finding services (payment processing, insurance, verification)? What would it need to look like (fixed subscription vs percentage of earnings, optional vs mandatory, reg on conditionality of additional services)?
4. Does our planned Stripe Connect flow (platform collects from client, deducts, pays carer) risk making us an "employment business" under the s.13(3) definition in force since January 2026, and if so what follows? Would destination charges/split payments where the carer's money never becomes ours change the analysis?
5. Given we are an employment agency in any event, which Conduct Regulations 2003 duties bite on a care introduction platform: work-seeker and hirer terms, identity and suitability checks for work with vulnerable people, advertising rules, transfer-fee rules for our off-platform introduction fee?
6. How do the incumbents (Curam, PrimeCarers, TrustonTap) sustain carer-side commission: is there a recognised legal analysis we are missing, or is it unaddressed non-compliance? What weight should we give to market practice?
7. What is the realistic enforcement exposure with the FWA: probability and sequence (warning letter, undertaking, order, prohibition, prosecution), personal liability of directors, and reputational risk of "naming"?
8. Is any fee we charge carers before the client-side restructure recoverable by carers as money paid under an unlawful demand, and would our carer contract terms be unenforceable to that extent?

## Product mitigations if the answer is bad

- **Flip the fee to the client side**: carer keeps 100% of their set rate; client pays rate plus a single platform fee of about 21% (economically identical to 15% + 6%). Hirer-side fees are unregulated. Show carers "you keep everything you charge" as a recruiting advantage.
- **Split payments at source**: use Stripe Connect destination charges so the carer's share never enters our balance and we only ever receive our own fee, reducing employment business and client-money concerns.
- **If a carer-side charge must exist**, make it a flat, optional subscription for defined non-work-finding services (enhanced profile, insurance bundle, payment tooling), never a percentage of earnings, never a condition of receiving introductions.
- **Comply as an employment agency regardless of the fee question**: written terms to carers and clients before services, identity and qualification checks appropriate for work with vulnerable adults, compliant advertising, and a compliant transfer-fee clause.
- **Paper trail**: record the solicitor's advice as an ADR in Docs/decisions/, and revisit if FWA publishes platform guidance or brings a first care-platform case.
