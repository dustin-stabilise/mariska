# Legal research overview: the six questions in one place

Synthesised: 2026-07-09 from the six per-question research files in this folder. This is research preparation for a solicitor meeting. It is NOT legal advice, and nothing here should be relied on as such. Where the per-question files and this overview differ, the per-question files (which carry the sources) win.

The six files:

1. `employment-agencies-act-commission.md` (the 15% carer-side commission vs the Employment Agencies Act 1973)
2. `platform-terms-structure.md` (terms of business, consumer law, pricing transparency)
3. `service-agreement-nonsolicitation.md` (the client-carer contract and circumvention fees)
4. `cqc-ongoing-role-payments.md` (staying outside CQC registration as an introduction agency)
5. `employment-status-guardrails.md` (keeping carers genuinely self-employed; tax and worker-status risk)
6. `payments-regulation-client-money.md` (FCA payment services perimeter, Stripe Connect, VAT)

## 1. The one-paragraph picture

The model (introduce self-employed carers to private families, contract formed directly between them, money via Stripe Connect, 15% carer-side commission plus 6% client-side fee) is the standard model in this market and every named competitor runs it openly. But the research found one genuine legal problem at its core: on the plain words of the Employment Agencies Act 1973 and the High Court's Simply Learning decision, deducting a work-finding fee from a carer's earnings is at real risk of being a criminal offence, and there is no exemption for care work. The incumbents appear to be protected by non-enforcement, not by a legal structure we can copy. The good news is that the cure is cheap and economically identical: put the whole margin on the client side. Everything else is defensible with careful product design: the CQC introduction-agency exclusion holds if we never direct or control the care, employment status holds if we keep the guardrails we already planned (carer sets rate, free decline), payments regulation is essentially solved by Stripe Connect, and the consumer-law and contract points are build-it-right work rather than existential risk. The solicitor meeting should be spent mostly on the fee structure and on the three places where payment handling touches other regimes (deemed-employer tax rules, the new "employment business" definition, and CQC's ongoing-charging example).

## 3. The solicitor agenda

Merged and deduplicated from the six files, ordered by importance. The per-file lists carry the full detail and statutory references.

1. **Is the 15% carer-side commission a prohibited fee under s.6 EAA 1973, and does flipping the whole margin to a single client-side fee of about 21% cure it?** This is the load-bearing question for the business model. Includes the anti-avoidance angle (the client fee being calculated off the carer's rate) and whether any residual carer-side charge could survive as a genuinely separate, optional, non-work-finding service.
2. **Does our Stripe Connect flow make us a deemed employer under s.44 ITEPA 2003?** The client will direct the carer in their own home, SDC by anyone counts, and the burden of proving no SDC sits on us; the key issue is whether the carer's pay is "under or in consequence of" a client-platform contract, and whether agent-for-collection or direct-charge structuring takes us outside it.
3. **Is an ongoing per-hour commission compatible with the CQC introduction-agency exclusion,** given CQC's guidance flags "continuing to charge" but the statutory test is direction or control of the care? Includes whether our payment handling can sit inside the "purely payroll function" carve-out and what itemisation that requires.
4. **Does the January 2026 "employment business" definition (participating includes "receiving payment for their services") catch a payment-handling marketplace,** and would destination charges vs direct charges on the carer's account change the answer? This is new law, untested, and touches files 1, 5 and 6 simultaneously.
5. **Assuming we are an employment agency regardless of the fee outcome, which Conduct Regulations 2003 duties bite:** reg 16 terms mechanics for carers, reg 22 checks for work with vulnerable people, advertising rules, and whether reg 10 transfer-fee limits (14 weeks / 8 weeks, extended-hire alternative) constrain our circumvention fee.
6. **Confirm the Stripe Connect payments analysis:** that gross payments transiting our platform balance under destination charges are never in our "possession or control", that no FCA authorisation or registration is needed, and which charge type is safest. Include the flows that could accidentally put money in our hands (refunds, adjustments, failed-card bank transfers).
7. **Design the circumvention fee to survive:** primary-obligation buy-out vs liquidated damages, quantum tied to lost commission vs fixed sum, defensible duration (case law supports about 6 months; market runs 12-24), and how much prominence at booking strengthens it against consumer-fairness attack.
8. **Where is the cumulative tipping point on employment-status control factors:** ratings, response-time expectations, mandatory training, badges; what substitution clause is realistic for vetted personal care; and can we restrict off-platform payment without recreating the Uber communication-control factor (which also collides with CQC's "requiring use of specific tools" example)?
9. **Consumer-law mechanics per booking:** which contract carries the 14-day cancellation right, who owes the pre-contract information duties, whether our reg 36 early-start capture is sufficient, and how "fully performed" reads for recurring weekly care.
10. **Confirm agent-not-principal for VAT and who invoices the client for the care element,** with drafting that simultaneously preserves the VAT agency analysis, the CQC introduction-agency position, and the never-holds-funds payments position.
11. **The client-as-employer problem:** HMRC's default for home carers is employment, usually by the client. Do we owe clients a warning, what wording is needed, and how do we handle marginal engagements (full-time, single-client, live-in)?
12. **Contract architecture:** one unified T&Cs or three documents; whether the client-carer template should give us third-party enforcement rights; liability cap sizing; and GDPR role allocation for health data (platform controller, carer independent controller, any joint controllership from shared features).
13. **Realistic enforcement exposure:** how the FWA and CQC actually behave toward this model, director personal liability, whether fees already taken from carers would be recoverable, and how much weight to give the incumbents' apparent impunity.
14. **Structural fallback timing:** whether a separate CQC-registered entity (the Curam pattern) is needed from day one or only when a feature crosses the line.

## 4. Do these before the solicitor meeting

No-regrets items the research already justifies. None gets worse whatever the solicitor says.

- **Freeze the carer-side deduction.** Do not launch or announce the 15% carer commission. Model and design the client-side single-fee alternative (carer keeps 100% of their set rate) so the flip is a config change, not a re-build. "You keep everything you charge" is also a carer-recruitment advantage.
- **Show fee-inclusive prices everywhere a price appears** (search cards, profiles, checkout), with the carer's own rate visible but secondary, and display the client fee VAT-inclusive from day one. Required by the DMCC Act regardless of everything else, and the CMA is actively enforcing.
- **Build the reg 36 checkout capture:** a per-booking express request to start within 14 days plus the acknowledgment of losing the cancellation right on full performance.
- **Adopt the hard payments rules now:** no client money into company bank accounts, no cash, all refunds and adjustments executed inside Stripe, no deposits or escrow in v1.
- **Lock the feature guardrails into the roadmap:** replacement carers only on the client's request (a button the client presses), no platform-arranged rotas, no care-quality monitoring or scoring, check-ins limited to satisfaction with the introduction service, ratings informational with no automatic consequences, no acceptance-rate or response-time penalties.
- **Copy discipline:** never say "we manage your care", "quality monitoring" or "guaranteed cover". Describe the platform as an introduction service with payment administration. Vetting and DBS claims in marketing become binding contract terms, so make them exactly true.
- **Circumvention-fee design defaults:** 6-12 months from the last paid booking (not 24 months from introduction), quantum tied to actual booking history, a published buy-out option, and plain-English prominence at booking rather than small print.
- **Template agreement:** include a carer public liability insurance requirement (the best market template omits it).

## 5. Cross-cutting tensions the files surfaced

- **The fee flip cures the EAA problem but not the CQC one.** A client-side fee escapes s.6 entirely, but CQC's red flag is "continuing to charge for the service being provided by a care worker" on either side. The CQC file's carve-outs (a one-off introduction fee payable in instalments; payment administration as a separately charged service) should shape how the flipped fee is structured and described. Design the restructure once, against both regimes.
- **Payment handling is the single thread running through four files.** Sitting in the money flow is what raises the s.44 deemed-employer question, the new "employment business" definition, the CQC ongoing-charging point, and the FCA perimeter. Stripe's direct-charges option (carer as merchant of record, our fee as an application fee) is the strongest answer to all four at once, at some UX cost. Ask the solicitor to evaluate the charge types against all four regimes together, not payments law alone.
- **Anti-disintermediation vs status and CQC.** Banning off-platform payment protects revenue but resembles Uber's control over the driver-passenger relationship (employment status) and CQC's "requiring care workers to use specific services" example. The files converge on the same alternative: retain through value (insurance, payment handling, cover carers valid only on-platform) plus a time-limited, transparent buy-out fee, rather than a prohibition.
- **Labelling cuts both ways.** For CQC and VAT we want the carer-side charge described as a separate payment-administration service; for the EAA, s.13(1) says a fee "includes any charge however described", so relabelling alone achieves nothing there. The description strategy must be consistent and honest across all documents, because a tribunal will look at substance (the Uber lesson applies to every regime here).
- **"Agent of both sides" is required by one regime and forbidden by another.** VAT and CQC positioning want us as agent for both client and carer; the payments commercial-agent exclusion is lost for exactly that reason. The resolution is that the exclusion does not matter while Stripe holds all funds, but this only stays true if the operational rules above are never broken.
- **Duration norms conflict.** Market circumvention tails run 12-24 months; the closest restraint-of-trade authority upheld 6 months; and if the Conduct Regulations apply, the statutory transfer-fee windows are 14 weeks from first supply or 8 weeks from the end of an assignment. Copying market practice here means copying its most vulnerable feature.
- **Market practice is evidence of tolerance, not legality.** All six files reach the same conclusion independently: Curam, PrimeCarers, TrustonTap and HomeTouch run the same structures, none engages publicly with the hard statutory points, and none has been enforced against. That is a fact worth putting to the solicitor, but never a safe harbour, and the enforcement landscape changed in 2026 (Fair Work Agency, CMA price-transparency enforcement, new safeguarding rules).
