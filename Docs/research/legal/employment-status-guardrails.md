# Self-employment status: keeping carers genuinely self-employed

Researched: 2026-07-09. This document prepares questions for a solicitor. It is NOT legal advice.

## 1. The status tests as HMRC and tribunals apply them

HMRC's framework is the ESM0500 series ("Guide to determining status"). The load-bearing elements [source: https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/employment-status-manual/esm0500]:

- **Personal service / substitution** (ESM0530 to ESM0538): a genuine right to send a substitute points away from employment; ESM0534 stresses the right "must be genuine", and ESM0537/0538 show HMRC will disprove or ignore a claimed right that is window dressing.
- **Mutuality of obligation** (ESM0543): "Without mutuality of obligation there can be no contract of any kind." The irreducible minimum is that the engager must be obliged to pay and the worker obliged to provide their own work or skill. ESM0543 cites the Supreme Court in PGMOL treating the wage-work bargain as "a pre-condition to employment", and Cornwall County Council v Prater: being "entirely free to work or not" does not stop someone being an employee at the times they are actually working. Nethermere v Gardiner: regular offer and acceptance of work over a period can create a continuous contract [source: https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/employment-status-manual/esm0543]. Practical upshot: "no obligation to offer or accept work" clauses protect against an umbrella contract but do NOT prevent employee or worker status during each individual booking.
- **Control** (ESM0516 to ESM0529): the right of control over what, where, when and how the work is done, whether or not exercised [source: https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/employment-status-manual/esm0500].

Tax status (employed vs self-employed) and employment-rights status (employee / limb (b) worker / self-employed) are separate determinations; the three-category rights framework is set out at gov.uk, where a "worker" is someone with "a contract or other arrangement to do work or services personally for a reward" and "only a limited right to send someone else to do the work" [source: https://www.gov.uk/employment-status/worker].

## 2. HMRC on domiciliary careworkers specifically (ESM4015)

ESM4015 is directly on point and is adverse as a starting position: "The case law tests normally indicate that a careworker who looks after a client in the client's home is likely to be an employee", because "there will often be a significant right of control, for example the carer required to arrive at a pre-arranged time" and perform tasks at the client's request. Facts may indicate self-employment where the careworker "looks after a number of people concurrently and has a business organisation in place". The assumed employer in that analysis is the client, not the introducer. Where an agency is involved and the agency legislation applies, separate rules take over (ESM2001 onwards) [source: https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/employment-status-manual/esm4015].

## 3. The platform-worker cases: what killed Uber, what saved Deliveroo

**Uber BV v Aslam [2021] UKSC 5.** The Supreme Court held drivers were limb (b) workers of Uber London. Two things matter for product design:

1. Contract labels do not decide status. Lord Leggatt: "it would be inconsistent with the purpose of this legislation to treat the terms of a written contract as the starting point" (para 76); the court takes a purposive approach and looks at the true agreement from all the circumstances (para 78) [source: https://caselaw.nationalarchives.gov.uk/uksc/2021/5].
2. The five findings of fact that established Uber's control (paras 94 to 101): (i) Uber fixed the fare, drivers could not charge more; (ii) Uber dictated the standard-form contract terms; (iii) once logged in, acceptance was constrained: information was withheld and drivers were penalised (automatic log-off) for declining too many trips; (iv) Uber controlled delivery of the service through routing, vehicle vetting and a ratings system used as internal performance management with termination for low scores; (v) Uber restricted driver-passenger communication and prevented any relationship beyond the individual trip [source: https://caselaw.nationalarchives.gov.uk/uksc/2021/5].

**Independent Workers Union of Great Britain v CAC (Deliveroo) [2023] UKSC 43.** Riders were held NOT to be in an employment relationship (Article 11 ECHR context) because of a genuine, virtually unfettered right of substitution: riders could appoint a substitute without approval, one rider ran a small margin business substituting out his slots, and Deliveroo did not object in practice. Personal service is an essential requirement of any employment relationship, so a genuine unfettered substitution right is fatal to worker status [source: https://www.bailii.org/uk/cases/UKSC/2023/43.html]. Law-firm commentary confirms the reading that the substitution right must be real and exercised without sanction, not merely written down (Macfarlanes, Nov 2023) [source: https://www.macfarlanes.com/what-we-think/2023/employment-status-and-the-right-of-substitution-deliveroo-drivers-not-workers-says-supreme-court/].

Read together: Uber lost on price-setting, penalised declines, performance management and communication control; Deliveroo won on genuine substitution and absence of subordination. Our model (carer sets the rate, free decline) is on the right side of the first list; substitution is our weakest limb because clients choose a named, vetted individual.

## 4. Who bears the risk if a carer is really a worker or employee

There are three distinct exposures. They can coexist.

**(a) The client as employer (the nanny analogy).** Gov.uk: you are usually classed as an employer if you hire someone to work in your home and "they're not self-employed or paid through an agency"; a person paying a carer or personal assistant directly is their employer even when funded by council direct payments or the NHS. Duties: register as employer, run PAYE, deduct income tax and NIC, employers' liability insurance, NMW, written statement, statutory sick/maternity pay, pension duties [source: https://www.gov.uk/au-pairs-employment-law]. This matches ESM4015's default. The realistic HMRC target here is the family, which is a serious product-trust problem even where the platform has no liability.

**(b) The platform as deemed employer for tax: the agency legislation (s.44 ITEPA 2003).** Post-2014, where a worker personally provides services to a client, there is a contract between client and agency under or in consequence of which the services are provided or paid for, and the worker is subject to (or to a right of) supervision, direction or control ("SDC") as to the manner of the work by ANYONE (including the client), the agency must operate PAYE/NIC as deemed employer [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2003/1/section/44 (s.44 conditions verified against the amended text) and https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/employment-status-manual/esm2000; note: the previously cited gov.uk publication "Employment intermediaries: personal services and supervision, direction or control" now redirects to ESM2000]. SDC can be exercised "by anyone... This includes the client, the agency, independent project managers, consultants, or other persons", and the evidential burden of showing no SDC sits on the agency [source: https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/employment-status-manual/esm2037]. Exclusions (ESM2032) do not obviously help: the "own home / premises not controlled by the client" exclusion fails because care must by its nature be performed in the client's home [source: https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/employment-status-manual/esm2032]. Because we collect the client's money and pay the carer through Stripe Connect, whether remuneration is "receivable under or in consequence of" a client-platform contract is the single most important tax question for the solicitor. This regime applies regardless of any CQC introduction-agency exemption: tax and regulation are independent.

**(c) The platform facing worker-status claims (the Uber route).** If a tribunal finds carers are limb (b) workers of the platform, the platform owes: National Minimum Wage, statutory paid holiday (5.6 weeks), rest breaks and the 48-hour average cap, protection from unlawful deductions and discrimination [source: https://www.gov.uk/employment-status/worker]. Pensions auto-enrolment also bites: the Pensions Act 2008 duty covers any individual who "has a contract to perform work or services personally and is not undertaking the work as part of their own business", and TPR states expressly that someone self-employed for tax "may still be classed as a 'worker'" for auto-enrolment [source: https://www.thepensionsregulator.gov.uk/en/document-library/automatic-enrolment-detailed-guidance/1-employer-duties-and-defining-the-workforce]. In Uber the consequence was NMW and paid holiday for all time logged in and available, not just booked time [source: https://caselaw.nationalarchives.gov.uk/uksc/2021/5]. PAYE, by contrast, follows tax status: limb (b) worker status alone does not create PAYE, but s.44 (above) or full employment status would.

## 5. IR35 / off-payroll working

IR35 (both the original Chapter 8 rules and the Chapter 10 off-payroll reform) only applies where the worker supplies services "through their own intermediary (usually a limited company, often known as a PSC)" [source: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/understanding-off-payroll-working-ir35]. Consequences for us:

- A sole-trader carer (the overwhelming norm) is outside IR35 entirely; the ordinary status tests in sections 1 to 4 govern, including s.44.
- Chapter 10 (client determines status, issues a status determination statement) applies to public authorities and medium/large businesses; a private individual receiving domestic care is not within it, and for small clients the worker's own intermediary decides status [source: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/understanding-off-payroll-working-ir35]. So a nurse operating through her own limited company must self-assess under Chapter 8; the family has no IR35 duty. Whether OUR platform contract could make US the "client" or fee-payer for any Chapter 10 analysis is a solicitor question, though the private-household nature of the end client points away from it.

## 6. Product guardrails: what protects status, what endangers it

Protective (map to the Uber factors and ESM tests):
- **Carer sets the hourly rate** (we do). Directly negates Uber factor (i). No platform price floors or caps, no "surge" overrides.
- **Unconditional right to decline** with no penalty, no acceptance-rate tracking, no ranking or search-visibility downgrade for declining. Negates Uber factor (iii).
- **No exclusivity**: carers free to work for other platforms and direct clients (during, not just after).
- **No supervision of the manner of care**: no platform care plans, no task checklists we author, no spot checks, no uniforms, no platform branding on the carer.
- **Substitution**: hardest to offer honestly in vetted personal care, but a clause allowing the carer to propose another platform-vetted carer as substitute (client may refuse a specific person, but the right is the carer's) is far stronger than nothing, per Deliveroo [source: https://www.bailii.org/uk/cases/UKSC/2023/43.html].
- **Payment framing**: invoice in the carer's name, platform described as payment collection agent for the carer; carer responsible for own tax; fees shown as commission on the carer's charge.
- **Direct relationship**: client and carer contract with each other, can communicate freely, and continuity with the same carer is a feature. Negates Uber factor (v).

Endangering (roughly in order of danger):
1. Penalising declines or slow responses (automatic delisting, ranking penalties, "response rate" badges used punitively): Uber factor (iii).
2. Platform-set prices, floors, or unilateral rate changes: Uber factor (i).
3. Ratings used as performance management with removal for low scores (a review system for client information is defensible; disciplinary use is not): Uber factor (iv).
4. Mandatory training delivered by us, mandatory care plans or methods: control over "how" (ESM0519 series) and evidence of SDC for s.44. Requiring proof of third-party qualifications and certificates at vetting is the safer framing.
5. Prohibiting off-platform contact or repeat direct bookings: Uber factor (v). (Note the tension: Curam prohibits off-platform payment; the solicitor should address how to protect revenue without recreating this factor.)
6. Guaranteed hours or minimum work: creates mutuality (ESM0543).

## Market practice

- **Curam** (T&Cs): carers are independent contractors, "not employees or workers of Curam for the purposes of the Employment Rights Act 1996, Working Time Regulations 1998 or any other employment legislation"; no mutuality of obligation; no guaranteed work; carers choose assignments, control how services are delivered, may work for third parties, handle their own tax; platform standards, monitoring, AI tools and payment systems stated not to create employment; all payment must run through Curam's systems, off-platform payment prohibited [source: https://www.curamcare.com/tscs].
- **PrimeCarers**: positions itself as an "introductory agency" that "does not provide, manage, supervise or clinically assess care"; carers are self-employed independent contractors who set their own hourly rate; the standard carer-client contract states nothing in it makes the carer "an employee, worker, agent or partner of the client or patient" [source: https://primecarers.co.uk/terms-of-service/contract] and [source: https://primecarers.co.uk/faq/carer]. PrimeCarers also publishes client-facing guidance on when a family employing a private carer directly becomes an employer [source: https://primecarers.co.uk/private-carers/employing-a-private-carer-legal-financial].
- Caveat: after Uber, such labels are only the paperwork; tribunals look at the true agreement [source: https://caselaw.nationalarchives.gov.uk/uksc/2021/5]. Market practice shows what is survivable commercially, not what is legally safe.

What remains genuinely open: the Employment Rights Act 2025 received Royal Assent on 18 December 2025 [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/36/contents]; the government's promised consultation on moving to a two-part status framework (worker vs self-employed, the "single worker status" idea) had not been published as at early 2026 and could redraw the limb (b) boundary [source: https://www.brownejacobson.com/insights/era-2025-revised-implementation-timeline-and-consultations]. No UK appellate case has yet decided the status of care-platform carers specifically. (PGMOL citation verified 2026-07-09: ESM0543 as published does cite PGMOL and describes mutuality/the wage-work bargain as "a pre-condition to employment".)

## Questions to put to the solicitor

1. Given Stripe Connect (client pays platform account, platform deducts 15% + 6%, pays carer), is the carer's remuneration "receivable under or in consequence of" a contract between the client and us for s.44 ITEPA 2003, making us deemed employer for PAYE/NIC? If yes, does restructuring payment as the carer's agent-for-collection, or direct client-to-carer payment with a separately invoiced platform fee, take us outside s.44?
2. For s.44 SDC "by anyone": the client will inevitably direct the carer in their own home. Does client-side direction alone satisfy SDC and sink us, and what evidence pack should we keep per worker to discharge the ESM2037 burden of proof?
3. What substitution clause is realistic for vetted personal care: is "carer may send another platform-vetted carer; client may veto an individual" a genuine unfettered right in the Deliveroo sense, or so conditional it counts for nothing?
4. Should our standard documents state that the client engages the carer directly, and if so how do we manage the resulting risk that the CLIENT is the employer (ESM4015 / nanny analysis)? What client-facing wording and warnings are needed, and do we owe the client any duty to warn them of possible employer obligations?
5. Are review/rating features, ID-badge requirements, mandatory annual training refreshers, and response-time expectations each individually survivable, and where is the cumulative tipping point on the Uber factors?
6. Can we prohibit or fee-charge off-platform bookings (like Curam) without recreating Uber factor (v) and exclusivity-style control? What is the least dangerous anti-disintermediation mechanism (e.g. one-off introduction fee)?
7. If a carer operates through her own limited company, do the Chapter 8 IR35 rules put any obligation on us or the family, and should we discourage PSCs on the platform?
8. Should the platform proactively register with any status determination (e.g. HMRC CEST runs per engagement template) and would a CEST output give us or the family any reasonable-care protection?
9. Worst case, if carers were held limb (b) workers of the platform: is exposure limited to booked hours or, per Uber, to all time available on the app; and how should we design availability features to avoid "logged in equals working time"?
10. Does the pending employment status consultation under the Employment Rights Act 2025 change any of the above answers within a 24-month horizon?

## Product mitigations if the answer is bad

- **s.44 payment risk**: restructure Stripe Connect so the platform is contractually the carer's payment collection agent (money is the carer's from receipt), or move to direct charges on the carer's connected account with a separate platform fee; worst case, operate PAYE as an agency for carers who fail the no-SDC evidence test.
- **Client-as-employer risk**: partner with or signpost a payroll bureau for clients; add a status questionnaire at booking; steer marginal engagements (full-time, single-client, live-in) to an employed model or off the platform.
- **Substitution**: build a real substitute flow (carer nominates a vetted stand-in, client can veto the person not the right), log every use, never penalise its exercise.
- **Control features**: keep ratings informational only, remove any automatic consequences; drop response-time and acceptance metrics from ranking; require certificates rather than delivering training; no uniforms, badges optional and carer-branded.
- **Mutuality**: no guaranteed hours products; recurring bookings framed as a series of separate engagements each of which either side may decline.
- **Anti-disintermediation**: replace an off-platform ban with a time-limited introduction fee if that is safer.
- **Structural fallback**: if genuine self-employment cannot be defended at scale, the fallback models are (a) employed-carer arm (Curam runs "Curam Managed Care Services Ltd" for managed engagements) or (b) pure introduction with one-off fees and no payment intermediation; both change unit economics and should be costed before launch.
