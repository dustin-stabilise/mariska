# The CQC line: when payment handling becomes a regulated "ongoing role"

Researched: 2026-07-09. This prepares questions for a solicitor. It is NOT legal advice.

## 1. The regulatory basis

- "Personal care" is a regulated activity under the Health and Social Care Act 2008 (Regulated Activities) Regulations 2014 (SI 2014/2936). Regulation 2 defines personal care as "(a) physical assistance given to a person in connection with (i) eating or drinking..., (ii) toileting..., (iii) washing or bathing, (iv) dressing, (v) oral care, or (vi) the care of skin, hair and nails", or "(b) the prompting, together with supervision, of a person, in relation to the performance of any of the activities listed in paragraph (a), where that person is unable to make a decision for themselves". The definition is unchanged since 2014 [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2014/2936/regulation/2].
- Schedule 1, paragraph 1 makes personal care regulated when provided "for persons who, by reason of old age, illness or disability are unable to provide it for themselves, and which is provided in a place where those persons are living". Paragraph 1(3) excludes, among others: "the supply of carers to a service provider by an undertaking acting as an employment agency or employment business" and, critically for us, "the introduction of carers to an individual (other than a service provider) by a person (including an employment agency or an employment business) having no ongoing role in the direction or control of the service provided to that individual" [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2014/2936/schedule/1].
- Carrying on a regulated activity without registration is a criminal offence under section 10 of the Health and Social Care Act 2008; penalties can include an unlimited fine and up to 12 months' imprisonment [source: https://www.stephensons.co.uk/site/blog/professional-discipline-blog/does-a-care-recruitment-agency-require-cqc-registration]. Verified against the statute: s.10(1) creates the offence, and the penalty on conviction is a fine and/or imprisonment for up to 12 months [source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2008/14/section/10].
- "Ongoing role in the direction or control" is not defined in the Regulations. Everything below the statute is CQC's interpretive guidance, not law.

## 2. CQC's published guidance on the ongoing role

CQC's guidance "Personal care: ongoing role, introductory agencies and individual care workers" (page last updated 26 May 2022; still the current text when fetched July 2026) states that "introductory agencies do not need to register with CQC as they do not have any ongoing role in the personal care that care workers provide to people after they are introduced" [source: https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-providers/registration/personal-care-ongoing-role-introductory-agencies-individual-care].

Examples CQC says "may constitute an ongoing role" requiring registration (guidance wording, not statute):

- "Monitoring the service provided to people by the care workers they have introduced", including "gathering feedback about the service from care workers and using feedback to advise or direct changes to how personal care is carried out".
- "Arranging rotas of care workers on behalf of people who receive care."
- "Being responsible for managing the replacement of a care worker. For example, by taking the initiative for providing absence cover in a way that goes beyond simply introducing another care worker, and in a way that does not need the person receiving care to make a decision."
- "Continuing to charge for the service being provided by a care worker after paying a one-off introduction fee. This does not include one-off fees paid in instalments and fees for payroll services."
- "Continuously reviewing and updating people's care needs when a person hasn't asked for this" and "making changes to active care plans".
- Requiring care workers to attend agency-supplied supervision/coaching, wear a uniform or badge, or "use specific paperwork, tools, equipment or services".

Examples CQC says do NOT amount to an ongoing role:

- Vetting/accreditation of carers before listing; needs assessments that carers (not the agency) use to plan work, provided the agency does "not monitor related outcomes or the care worker's performance".
- Signposting general training; advice on tax, health and safety and regulation; "charging people a 'one-off' fee for introductions".
- "Contacting the person receiving care to make sure they are satisfied with the introductory service provided", so long as this does not gather information about the care itself or direct the carer.
- "Introducing additional or replacement care workers where a person receiving care asks for this."
- "Carrying out a purely payroll function for people who employ introduced workers. This would be an additional, separately charged administrative/business service."

All quotations: [source: https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-providers/registration/personal-care-ongoing-role-introductory-agencies-individual-care]. No 2023-2026 update to this guidance was found; CQC's broader Scope of Registration page was last updated 14 May 2026 but simply points back to Regulation 2 and Schedule 1(1) [source: https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/registration/scope-registration/regulated-activities/personal-care].

## 3. Does the Stripe Connect payment model cross the line?

What the guidance actually says about money:

- The only payment-related "ongoing role" example is "continuing to charge for the service being provided by a care worker after paying a one-off introduction fee", with two carve-outs: one-off fees paid in instalments, and "fees for payroll services" [source: https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-providers/registration/personal-care-ongoing-role-introductory-agencies-individual-care].
- A "purely payroll function" is expressly permitted, framed as "an additional, separately charged administrative/business service" [same source].

The tension for our model: a 15% carer-side commission plus 6% client-side fee levied per hour of care booked is, on a literal reading, "continuing to charge for the service being provided by a care worker", not a one-off introduction fee. Nothing in the guidance says an ongoing percentage commission is safe. What the guidance also does NOT say is that collecting the client's money, deducting a fee, and paying the carer is itself "direction or control" of the care: the statutory test is about the service provided, i.e. the care, and payment handling does not direct how care is delivered. This gap between the guidance example (ongoing charging = red flag) and the statutory test (direction or control of the care) is the central question for the solicitor. No court or tribunal decision interpreting "ongoing role in the direction or control" for an introduction agency was found in this research (searched CQC, tribunal and law-firm sources); treat the point as untested.

Adjacent tools per the guidance: timesheets and in-platform invoicing are not mentioned by name; the closest hooks are the payroll carve-out (helpful) and the "requiring care workers to use specific paperwork, tools, equipment or services" example (unhelpful, since we mandate in-platform booking and payment). Rota arranging and platform-managed replacement are named as ongoing-role examples. Satisfaction check-ins are fine only if confined to the introduction service, not the care.

## 4. Enforcement picture (2023-2026)

- No published CQC enforcement action against an introduction agency for its payment handling was found. Published prosecutions target businesses actually delivering personal care unregistered: e.g. Golden Years Caring at Home Ltd (Cosham, Portsmouth), whose directors were ordered to pay £44,003 in total (£34,833 fine, £9,000 costs, £170 victim surcharge) in June 2020 for operating a homecare service without registration [source: https://www.cqc.org.uk/news/releases/unregistered-provider-fined-%C2%A344003-after-cqc-brings-prosecution] (the CQC release does not name the court; "Portsmouth Magistrates' Court" could not be verified); Five Star Homecare Leeds Ltd, whose owner was fined £1,500 plus £10,380.91 costs and a £170 surcharge (just over £12,000) at Leeds Magistrates' Court in January 2021 [source: https://www.homecareinsight.co.uk/home-care-provider-ordered-to-pay-12k-after-faking-cqc-registration/].
- CORRECTED: the previously cited "four prosecutions of unregistered businesses between 2023 and 2024" figure does not appear on the Acuity Law page (which reports over 100 CQC prosecutions across a 14-year period [source: https://acuitylaw.com/cqc-criminal-prosecutions/]). Mills & Reeve (May 2024) reports 21 CQC prosecutions in 2022, 25 in 2023 and four in early 2024, across all offence types, not only unregistered providers [source: https://www.mills-reeve.com/blogs/health-and-care/may-2024/are-cqc-prosecutions-on-the-rise/]. CQC also issues fixed penalty notices as an alternative to prosecution; its FPN page (updated May 2026) publishes each notice for only three months, so the public record understates activity [source: https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/enforcement/penalty-notices-unregistered-providers].
- CQC's regulatory sandbox round 3 (2019) examined "umbrella organisations" that match care workers with clients, support agreement development and "monitor the quality and safety of the service being provided", explicitly because "the way we regulate does not always fit their business model"; the round was later put on hold and no new service type has been published [source: https://www.cqc.org.uk/what-we-do/how-we-work-people/regulatory-sandbox-round-3-community-care-home-teams]. Bevan Brittan (December 2019) noted such organisations operate "at the very boundary of the requirement for registration" [source: https://www.bevanbrittan.com/insights/articles/2019/cqc-registration-of-agencies/].

## 5. Market practice: how comparable platforms describe their payment role

- **Curam** collects payment from the client, holds it, and releases it to the carer (card payments "released to care workers usually 2 working days after a shift ends") minus its fee; it currently describes a 15% carer-side fee plus a 12% client fee added to invoices [source: https://www.curamcare.com/client-information]. Its terms state that a Service Agreement "forms a contract directly between the client and the carer", that "all payments must be processed via Curam's authorised systems", and that nothing in its tools, "monitoring systems", insurance access or safeguarding processes "shall be interpreted as Curam exercising supervision, direction or control over carers" [source: https://www.curamcare.com/tscs]. Curam has separately launched CQC-regulated care packages through group companies, alongside the unregulated introductory model [source: https://www.curamcare.com/client-information].
- **PrimeCarers** states it is "classified as an 'introductory agency'" per CQC guidance, does not "supply carers... employ carers... [or] exercise control over the pricing or delivery of care", requires that "all payments for work completed must be made via PrimeCarers", and builds its commission into the carer's displayed rate [source: https://primecarers.co.uk/terms-of-service/client]. Note: its terms also advertise "monitoring tools for customers to monitor care activity" and say it will "endeavour to find a replacement carer where at all possible" after complaints; both sit close to CQC's ongoing-role examples, and market practice is not a safe harbour.
- **TrustonTap** states "TrustonTap is defined as an introductory agency by the Care Quality Commission (CQC) and is not required to register", and, notably, that because of this it cannot direct care changes, recommend visit-frequency increases, or replace care workers. It invoices clients weekly by Direct Debit against carer-completed timesheets and deducts a commission before paying the self-employed carer [source: https://www.trustontap.com/care-services/frequently-asked-questions/].

Market practice is therefore uniform: platform-mediated payment, per-hour commission, timesheet-driven invoicing, carer sets the rate, contract is client-carer. CQC has not (publicly) moved against this model. That is evidence of tolerated practice, not of legality.

## 6. Roadmap features ranked by risk against the guidance

1. **Backup-carer pools / platform-initiated replacement (HIGH).** "Being responsible for managing the replacement of a care worker... in a way that does not need the person receiving care to make a decision" is a named ongoing-role example. Any automatic cover feature must instead surface options for the client to choose.
2. **Monitoring check-ins about the care (HIGH).** Gathering feedback about the care and using it "to advise or direct changes" is a named example. Check-ins must be limited to satisfaction with the introduction service.
3. **Rota tools operated by the platform (HIGH).** "Arranging rotas of care workers on behalf of people who receive care" is a named example. Client-driven scheduling where the client (or carer) builds the rota is the distinction to preserve.
4. **Care notes (MEDIUM-HIGH).** Not named, but if the platform reads, reviews or acts on care notes it becomes "monitoring the service"; if notes pass client-to-carer as a private record the platform does not use, the risk drops. Also caught by "requiring care workers to use specific paperwork, tools, equipment or services" if mandatory.
5. **Ongoing percentage commission (MEDIUM, but foundational).** Literally within "continuing to charge for the service being provided by a care worker", yet universal market practice and arguably not "direction or control" of the care. The single most important solicitor question.
6. **Visit confirmations / timesheets for billing (MEDIUM-LOW).** Closest to the permitted "purely payroll function" when used solely to compute payment; risk rises if confirmations feed monitoring, quality scoring or alerts.
7. **Replacement search at the client's request (LOW).** "Introducing additional or replacement care workers where a person receiving care asks for this" is expressly permitted.
All feature quotations: [source: https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-providers/registration/personal-care-ongoing-role-introductory-agencies-individual-care].

## Questions to put to the solicitor

1. Is an ongoing per-hour commission (15% carer-side plus 6% client-side) within the introduction-agency exclusion in Schedule 1, para 1(3), given CQC's guidance lists "continuing to charge for the service being provided by a care worker" as an ongoing-role example but the statutory test is "direction or control of the service provided"?
2. Does collecting the client's money via Stripe Connect, deducting fees and paying the carer count as a "purely payroll function"? Does the guidance's requirement that payroll be "an additional, separately charged administrative/business service" mean our commission must be itemised separately from a payment-processing fee?
3. CQC's guidance carves out "one-off fees paid in instalments". Could our client-side 6% fee be structured or described as an introduction fee collected in instalments, and would that change anything in substance?
4. Does making in-platform booking, invoicing and payment mandatory (with sanctions for off-platform payment, as Curam and PrimeCarers do) fall within "requiring care workers to use specific paperwork, tools, equipment or services"?
5. If carers write care notes in the platform that the platform never reviews or acts on, is hosting them "monitoring the service"? What technical or contractual separation (e.g. no staff access absent a safeguarding report) would the solicitor want to see?
6. Where is the line on visit confirmations: is a GPS or tap-to-confirm record used purely to trigger payment release acceptable, and at what point (missed-visit alerts to the platform, escalation workflows) does it become monitoring?
7. Is a client-facing "find a replacement" button that the client must press acceptable, and can the platform pre-notify a pool of backup carers of a likely absence before the client decides?
8. Is there any case law, tribunal decision or CQC enforcement position on "ongoing role in the direction or control" beyond the published guidance, and what weight does the guidance carry?
9. Should we follow Curam's structure of a separate CQC-registered group entity for managed care from day one, or only if a feature crosses the line?
10. What written records (statement of purpose, terms wording, feature specifications) would best evidence the no-ongoing-role position if CQC ever queried us pre-launch?

## Product mitigations if the answer is bad

- **Commission structure:** re-paper the client-side fee as a one-off introduction fee per engagement, payable in instalments (expressly permitted), and itemise the carer-side deduction as a separately charged payroll/payment-administration service.
- **Payments:** keep Stripe Connect but move to a pure pass-through description: carer invoices client, platform processes payment as agent of the carer, payroll fee shown as a separate line.
- **Replacement/backup pools:** ship only a client-initiated flow (client requests, platform introduces candidates, client selects). No automatic cover.
- **Monitoring/check-ins:** restrict automated check-ins to platform-service satisfaction; route any care-quality feedback client-to-carer without platform reading or scoring it.
- **Care notes and rotas:** make them client-owned tools the client and carer edit; no platform review, no platform-generated care plans; scheduling proposed by carer or client, never assigned by the platform.
- **Structural fallback:** register a separate entity with CQC for the regulated activity of personal care (Curam's model) and route any feature that requires direction or control through it; or drop the feature.
- **Copy discipline:** never market "we manage your care", "quality monitoring" or "guaranteed cover"; describe the platform as an introduction service with payment administration.
