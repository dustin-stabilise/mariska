# homecare.co.uk — Competitive Intelligence Report

**One-liner:** The UK's dominant reviews-and-listings directory for home care — a media/SaaS business (part of Tomorrow's Guides, which also runs carehome.co.uk and daynurseries.co.uk) that monetises care providers via annual advertising subscriptions while staying completely free for families.

---

## 1. Business model

- **Who pays whom:** Care *providers* (agencies) pay homecare.co.uk; families pay nothing. The site states it is "completely free for families to use" (https://www.homecare.co.uk/about/) and explicitly: "we are not a care provider, nor do we own or are partnered with any home care agencies" (homepage/FAQ, surfaced via Trustpilot research).
- **Revenue streams:**
  1. **Provider advertising subscriptions** (Enhanced / Platinum annual tiers — see Pricing). Over 3,000 of ~13,000 listed home care providers subscribe (https://www.homecare.co.uk/our-services/services). Every UK provider gets a free basic listing by default (https://support.homecare.co.uk/docs/homecare-co-uk-overview).
  2. **Recruitment/jobs board** — job posting credits are bundled into subscriptions (10 credits Enhanced, 50 Platinum) and Platinum adds CV-database search; ~1,947 live jobs and 42,211 registered jobseekers (homepage, https://www.homecare.co.uk/jobs/).
  3. **Reviews SaaS** — parent Tomorrow's Guides describes itself as "website publishers and marketing & reviews software as a service (SaaS) providers for the Care Home, Home Care and Nursery sectors" (https://www.tomorrows.co.uk/).
- **Marketplace vs managed:** Neither. It is a pure directory/media business — no matching, no transactions, no take rate, no commission. Providers pay flat subscriptions regardless of lead volume; families contact providers directly (phone/email/consultation request) and all care arrangements happen off-platform.
- **Take rate:** N/A — flat-fee subscription model. Their own ROI claim: Platinum delivers "an average return of £25 for every £1 invested" (https://www.homecare.co.uk/our-services/services).

## 2. Pricing

**For families:** Free. No fees anywhere in the family journey. Provider *care* prices are displayed on listings (a differentiator — most competitors hide pricing).

**For providers (the paying side)** — from https://www.homecare.co.uk/our-services/services:
- **Basic listing:** Free (every UK provider auto-listed).
- **Enhanced:** **£34.50 + VAT/month** (billed annually, £414 + VAT/yr) — full profile, ranks above basic listings, call insights, website referrals, consultation bookings, review tools, 10 job credits.
- **Platinum (recommended):** **£69 + VAT/month** (£828 + VAT/yr) — everything in Enhanced plus ranking boost, email/call channelling, branded review cards, review showcase, 50 job credits, CV search.
- Claims: enquiries up 18% in the last 12 months; "60% of people who enquire through homecare.co.uk find care within 2 months"; call recording/summaries included at no extra cost.
- Sales funnel for providers: phone (01488 684321) or callback request — annual contract, "no hidden costs."

**Market price data they publish** (useful benchmark for CareMatch's own content): UK home care averages **£26–£38/hour**; live-in care **£900–£1,400/week**, up to £2,000/week (https://www.homecare.co.uk/advice/paying-for-care-at-home).

## 3. Client (family) acquisition

- **SEO is the engine.** Massive programmatic footprint: listing pages for every country, county (~100+ county URLs like `/homecare/listings.cfm/searchcounty/Berkshire`), district, postal town, postcode, and care type (`/hctype/live-in-care`, `dementia-care`, `nursing-care`, `visiting-care`). This structure targets every "[home care] + [place]" and "[care type] + [place]" query in the UK.
- **Content/advice hub:** Deep advice library ("Paying for care at home", "Care needs assessment", "Types of homecare", benefits guides, dementia guides) targeting top-of-funnel informational queries — pages carry year-stamped titles ("Cost of home care services 2026") for freshness signals.
- **Scale:** ~168,000 monthly visits to home care listings / ~3M visits/year for homecare.co.uk alone (https://www.homecare.co.uk/about/, https://support.homecare.co.uk/docs/homecare-co-uk-overview); parent claims 22.5M combined annual visitors across its three sites and that ">70% of research on older-care discovery websites" happens on carehome.co.uk + homecare.co.uk combined (https://www.tomorrows.co.uk/, about page — *their own claim, not independently verified*).
- **Reviews as acquisition flywheel:** 98,651 "verified reviews" (homepage, July 2026) generate unique long-tail content, and providers are given branded review cards / embeddable widgets that link back — turning 3,000+ paying providers into a distributed backlink and review-solicitation network (https://support.homecare.co.uk/docs/homecare-co-uk-overview).
- **Awards as PR:** Annual "Top 20 Home Care Awards" by region + Home Care Group Awards. Winners display badges on their own sites and press-release the win — free links and brand mentions every year.
- **Paid ads / partnerships:** No evidence found of significant paid acquisition or formal council/NHS/hospital-discharge partnerships. Their moat is organic. (*Absence of evidence — inferred, not confirmed.*)
- **15+ years of domain authority** on an exact-match domain — effectively unassailable head-term SEO position.

## 4. Carer supply side

homecare.co.uk does **not** recruit or vet carers — it has no carer marketplace. Its "supply side" is:
- **Agencies (the customer):** free auto-listing, then upsell to subscriptions. Onboarding friction is low (profile already exists; claim it and enrich it).
- **Care workers (jobs board):** free for candidates — CV registration (42,211 registered), job alerts, applications across 1,947 live roles (https://www.homecare.co.uk/jobs/, homepage). This is a recruitment channel *for its agency customers*, not a carer network. Carer-facing pitch is thin — generic job-board messaging.
- No vetting, DBS checking, or carer earnings involvement of any kind. Carers' pay is between them and the employing agency.
- **Implication:** the CV database (Platinum feature) is a notable asset — they monetise the same audience twice (families + recruitment).

## 5. Website/product teardown

- **Positioning/headline:** "Find the right home care" — "compare types of care, prices and reviews for all UK home care providers"; "The UK's most complete guide to home care" (homepage). Pure comparison/trust positioning, not matching or speed.
- **Conversion funnel (family):** Location search box → listings page (ranked, with note "Ranking affected by subscription level"; 3 featured/promoted providers shown above the organic list) → provider profile → contact provider directly: displayed phone number (tracked for subscriber call-insights), "Send Email," "Request Consultation," "Check Availability," or click out to the provider's website. No account required to contact; accounts enable shortlists.
- **Listing cards show:** review score (e.g. 9.8/10) + review count, **starting prices** (hourly visiting rate, weekly live-in rate), award badges, photo/video counts, care-type icons, save-to-shortlist.
- **Provider profile depth** (example: Ashridge Home Care, `agency.cfm/id/65432219881`): 9.8/10 from 68 reviews with recency breakdown; exact fees (£38.48–£39.90/hr; £1,700–£2,050/wk live-in); funding types accepted; **CQC rating displayed** ("Good", "Outstanding" in Caring); 26 awards; 9 staff bios; carer headcount; "Enhanced Service" subscriber badge.
- **Trust signals:** review-verification methodology page ("Real people, Recent experiences, No conflicts of interest, Individually verified"); Review Score algorithm (1–10, weights recent reviews); CQC ratings surfaced per provider; awards; "15+ years." Review integrity process is genuinely robust: automated checks (phone/IP/duplicate detection), human review team "overseen by lawyers," reviewer authentication calls, and providers **cannot block publication** — a pending review auto-publishes after 7 days; provider's only options are publish-now or publish-with-reply (https://support.homecare.co.uk/docs/our-review-publishing-process).
- **Notable product features:** call tracking + recording/AI summaries for subscribers; provider analytics/benchmarking dashboards; embeddable review widgets; consultation-booking; shortlists. **Absent:** matching algorithm, in-platform messaging, payments, care plans, carer profiles — none of the marketplace layer.
- **Tech note:** front end recently rebuilt on Astro (`/_astro/` assets) over a legacy ColdFusion back end (`.cfm` URLs) — long-lived platform being modernised (*observed from page source*).

## 6. Regulatory stance

- **Neither a CQC provider nor an introduction agency.** Pure information/advertising platform: "we are not a care provider, nor do we own or are partnered with any home care agencies." It never touches care delivery, employment, or introductions, so it sits entirely outside CQC scope — while *leaning on* CQC by displaying each agency's CQC rating as a trust signal.
- This is the lightest-possible regulatory posture in the sector and part of why the model is so profitable/low-risk compared with managed providers (Cera) or introduction agencies (CareMatch's model).

## 7. Traction & financials

- **Parent:** Tomorrow's Guides Limited, company no. 02889550, incorporated **20 Jan 1994**, Hungerford, Berkshire; SIC 58190 "Other publishing activities"; active, accounts to 31 Dec 2024 filed (https://find-and-update.company-information.service.gov.uk/company/02889550). An earlier HOMECARE.CO.UK LIMITED (04432503, inc. 2002) was dissolved 2013 — the brand predates the current corporate vehicle's focus. homecare.co.uk itself claims "15+ years," implying a ~2009–2010 launch (*inferred*).
- **Scale signals:** 124 employees; 15,000+ paying customers across care + nursery sectors; 22.5M combined annual visitors (https://www.tomorrows.co.uk/, https://theorg.com/org/tomorrows-guides).
- **Funding:** No evidence of external/PE investment found — appears founder-owned and bootstrapped over 30 years (*inferred from absence of any deal news; PitchBook profile exists but no disclosed rounds*).
- **Revenue:** Not public (private company; figures not in search-accessible filings). Rough sizing: 3,000+ home care subscribers × £414–£828/yr suggests **£1.5–2.5M/yr from homecare.co.uk subscriptions alone**, with carehome.co.uk (the larger site, higher-value care-home customers) plus daynurseries plausibly putting group revenue at £10M+ (*back-of-envelope estimate — treat as illustrative only*).
- **Growth signals:** listings grew from ~9,000 providers/18,000 reviews (older FAQ copy) to 13,149 providers/98,651 reviews (July 2026 homepage); enquiries claimed +18% YoY. Steady compounding, not hypergrowth.
- **Reputation risk signal:** Trustpilot rating only **2.8/5 (21 reviews)** — mostly *providers* complaining about subscription ROI ("not received a single referral") and review-moderation disputes (https://uk.trustpilot.com/review/homecare.co.uk). Families barely review the platform itself — consistent with it being a pass-through directory.

## 8. Strengths, weaknesses, lessons for CareMatch

**Strengths**
- Near-monopoly SEO position on UK home-care discovery queries; 30 years of domain authority; programmatic geo/care-type page architecture.
- Review corpus (98k+) is a self-reinforcing moat: more reviews → better rankings → more families → providers must be present → providers solicit more reviews.
- Zero regulatory exposure, zero care-delivery cost, flat-fee SaaS revenue — extremely capital-efficient.
- Price transparency (real £/hr on listings) that most of the industry avoids.
- Legally hardened review process providers cannot veto — credibility with families.

**Weaknesses**
- No transaction layer: it hands the family off at the phone call. No matching, messaging, booking, payments, or outcome data — the entire "arrange care" job is unserved.
- Ranking is pay-to-play ("Ranking affected by subscription level") — a quiet conflict with its neutral-guide positioning.
- Only agencies are listed; the growing self-employed-carer/introduction segment (CareMatch's market) is structurally invisible on it.
- Provider-side NPS looks shaky (2.8/5 Trustpilot; ROI complaints from small providers) — smaller agencies may churn.
- Legacy tech and flat-fee model leave money on the table vs. per-lead or per-placement pricing.

**Lessons for CareMatch**
1. **Be on it, cheaply, early.** If CareMatch (or its carers operating as an entity) can qualify for a listing, a free/Enhanced (£414+VAT/yr) presence is among the cheapest warm-lead channels in UK care; their own claim is 60% of enquirers find care within 2 months — these are high-intent families. Verify eligibility rules for introduction agencies first (*uncertain whether non-CQC introducers are listed — several introduction platforms do appear on it; confirm directly*).
2. **Copy the programmatic SEO architecture, not just the content.** County/town/postcode × care-type landing pages plus a year-stamped advice hub is exactly how they compound organic traffic. CareMatch can replicate this per-carer/per-area with richer, marketplace-native pages homecare.co.uk can't build (individual carer profiles with availability and instant booking).
3. **Reviews only convert when verification is visibly rigorous.** Their 7-day, lawyer-overseen, provider-can't-block process is the trust template. Build review verification into CareMatch v1 (verified-placement-only reviews) — it's the single biggest trust asset a marketplace can own.
4. **Their gap is CareMatch's product.** They monetise *attention*; they abandon the user at the hardest moment (choosing, contacting, arranging, paying). A marketplace that closes the loop — match → message → book → pay, with DBS/insurance verification — is complementary today and a disruptive substitute tomorrow. Price transparency on every profile is table stakes; they've already trained families to expect £/hr on listing cards.
5. **Flat-fee subscription is the wrong model for supply that needs leads, but note why they chose it:** it avoids lead-quality disputes and CQC/agency-fee entanglement, and it made them profitable without VC. If CareMatch charges carers, watch their Trustpilot failure mode: small suppliers who pay and get zero leads become vocal detractors — tie any supply-side fee to delivered value (per-placement or success-fee), or keep supply free and charge families.
