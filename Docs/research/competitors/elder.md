# Elder (elder.org) — Competitive Intelligence Report

**One-liner:** The UK's best-funded introductory live-in care platform — legally an introduction agency using self-employed carers, but packaged and sold as a managed, concierge-style service at a premium price with an inferred ~35-40% take rate.

---

## 1. Business model

- **Legal model:** Introduction agency. Their own site states: "The Care Quality Commission (CQC) defines companies like Elder as an introductory agency pursuant to the Health & Social Care Act 2008" ([elder.org](https://www.elder.org/), [elder.org/live-in-care](https://www.elder.org/live-in-care/)). Carers are self-employed; families contract with the carer, not Elder.
- **Operating model:** Despite the introduction-agency legal wrapper, Elder behaves like a managed service:
  - Elder — not the carer — sets the price on each placement. Their carer documentation says carers "should be able to see the weekly rate on the placement advert before you apply" and that Elder "regularly review[s] the market" on rates ([elder.org/documentation/what-self-employed-carers-can-expect-from-elder](https://www.elder.org/documentation/what-self-employed-carers-can-expect-from-elder/)). This is a key structural difference from true open marketplaces like Curam, where carers set their own rates.
  - Elder handles payment processing, matching/shortlisting, 24/7 support, respite cover arrangements, and a care-management portal (MyElder) ([elder.org/live-in-care](https://www.elder.org/live-in-care/)).
  - Elder carries £5M public liability insurance, which most pure introducers do not ([eldering.co.uk comparison](https://eldering.co.uk/best-uk-introductory-live-in-care-agencies/)).
- **Who pays whom:** Family pays Elder an all-inclusive weekly rate; Elder pays the carer their placement rate. Revenue = the spread (agency fee, "variable depending on need, plus VAT" per [eldering.co.uk](https://eldering.co.uk/best-uk-introductory-live-in-care-agencies/)). No joining fees, no exit fees, no carer subscription found.
- **Take rate (inferred, not disclosed):** Family side starts "from £1,210 per week" ([elder.org/live-in-care](https://www.elder.org/live-in-care/)); carer side "the average live-in care placement on Elder's platform offers £720 a week" ([elder.org/become-a-carer](https://www.elder.org/become-a-carer/)). Comparing the £720 average carer payout to even the floor family price implies a spread of roughly **£490/week ≈ 40%** — consistent with the 26-40% range cited in sector commentary. **Inference:** exact take varies per placement (complexity, region) and Elder never publishes a percentage; carer reviews describe it as "one of the greediest commissions" (carer review themes surfaced via Glassdoor/Indeed search snippets).

## 2. Pricing

**Family side** (all from [elder.org/pricing](https://www.elder.org/pricing/) unless noted):
- **Live-in care:** "From £1,210 p/w" (homepage/landing pages also cite "from £1,150" in some guides — likely regional/legacy copy; treat £1,210 as current headline). Includes a trial period ("two-week trial" on pricing page; the live-in page says "14-day trial period with no deposits or joining fees").
- **Respite care:** "3 days from £684" (=£228/day) or "From £1,595 p/w"; 3-day minimum.
- **Visiting care:** offered "from as little as 1 hour per week" ([elder.org homepage](https://www.elder.org/)) — no family-side hourly price published on the pages fetched (uncertain).
- **Add-ons:** carer with vehicle £40–£80/week; caring for a spouse/couple +£80/week; managed food budget £40/week.
- **Included:** "No weekend or bank holiday surcharge", "2x nightly wake-ups included", "No lengthy contracts", "No joining fees", two weeks' notice to end.
- **Funding narrative:** heavy content steering families to local-authority funding, direct payments, and NHS Continuing Healthcare ("covers 100% of your fees if you have a primary health need") ([elder.org/pricing](https://www.elder.org/pricing/), [elder.org/nhs-continuing-healthcare-funding](https://www.elder.org/nhs-continuing-healthcare-funding/)).
- **Funnel behaviour:** prices ARE published (unlike many competitors) but the primary CTA is still "Find a carer" → care-request form, plus a prominent phone line (0333 920 3648) and "care advisors" — a sales-assisted funnel behind a self-serve front.

**Carer side** (from [elder.org/become-a-carer](https://www.elder.org/become-a-carer/) and [carer documentation](https://www.elder.org/documentation/what-self-employed-carers-can-expect-from-elder/)):
- Average live-in placement pays **£720/week**; visiting care "up to £20 per hour".
- Rates are set per-placement by Elder; higher for couples or night care; **double pay on bank holidays**; travel costs to/from placement covered; pay prorated for partial weeks.
- No sign-up fee or explicit commission charged to carers (the fee is embedded in the spread, invisible to both sides).
- Perks: Onsi loyalty programme (sick pay, mental-health support, e-learning) — from carer-facing content surfaced in search.

## 3. Client acquisition

- **SEO is the dominant visible channel.** Elder runs a very large content library across multiple hubs: `/articles/paying-for-care/` (e.g. "How much do carers cost?", "How much do care agencies charge per hour?"), `/paying-for-care/`, `/arrange-care/` (e.g. "6 weeks free NHS intermediate care — what happens next?", hospital-discharge guides), condition-specific dementia/mobility content, and a flagship "Ultimate guide: NHS continuing healthcare funding". They rank for high-intent commercial queries ("live-in care cost UK") with their own pages occupying multiple SERP slots. Pattern: intercept families at the *funding-confusion* and *hospital-discharge* moments, not just "hire a carer" queries.
- **Landing-page variants** exist for paid/organic testing (e.g. `elder.org/elder-live-in-care` duplicate landing page — typical of paid-search campaigns). **Inference:** they run paid search; not directly verified.
- **Phone-first conversion assist:** a national 0333 number is on every page; "care advisors" handle inbound — classic high-AOV assisted-sales model.
- **Partnerships:** No verified formal NHS/council contracts found. Their content targets hospital-discharge and CHC-funding moments, which functions as *de facto* discharge-channel acquisition via SEO rather than formal partnership (uncertain — they may have B2B referral relationships not visible publicly).
- **PR/awards:** Leaders in Care Awards 2025, Health Investor Awards 2023, Home Care Awards 2022, Deloitte Technology Fast 50 (2020) — displayed as trust badges ([elder.org](https://www.elder.org/)).
- **Review platforms:** Trustpilot actively cultivated (4.7 "Excellent", 750+ reviews, per [trustpilot.com/review/elder.org](https://www.trustpilot.com/review/elder.org) search snippets).

## 4. Carer supply side

- **Pitch:** flexibility ("pick and choose work that fits around your life"), real-time job notifications, on-time payment, 24/7 support, break booking ([elder.org/become-a-carer](https://www.elder.org/become-a-carer/)).
- **Onboarding funnel:** (1) 10-minute online questionnaire → (2) phone screening call → (3) create profile and apply to placements. Relatively low friction, but with a hard selectivity claim: **"Only 2% of applicants to join Elder are successful"** ([elder.org](https://www.elder.org/)).
- **Vetting requirements:** Enhanced DBS (England/Wales), professional care references (care-home or domiciliary background required — no untrained carers), right to work, photo ID, good English; live-in carers must commit to ~7-day residential stints.
- **Carer sentiment (mixed-to-negative):** search-surfaced review themes include "relatively low rates of pay", "terrible communication from head office, no direct line for carer support", and "one of the greediest commissions" (Glassdoor/Indeed snippets; pages themselves blocked 403, so treat as indicative rather than quantified). Elder publicly responds that it "continuously review[s] carer pay" and raised rates in November (year unspecified).
- **Structural tension:** carers can't set prices or negotiate directly, yet bear self-employment risk. Tax commentators have flagged employment-status/IR35 exposure for introductory agencies operating this way ([taxadvisermagazine.com article](https://www.taxadvisermagazine.com/article/introductory-agencies-and-self-employed-live-carers) — content not retrievable, 403; flag as a known sector debate rather than a verified claim about Elder).

## 5. Website / product teardown

- **Positioning:** "Find the right carer and keep the life you know and love" — anti-care-home framing ("displace the care home" was the Series B narrative per [Acton Capital](https://www.actoncapital.com/news/high-growth-startup-elder-raises-further-ps8-2m-11m-in-bid-to-displace-the-care-home)). Price anchor: "services facilitated by Elder are 35% cheaper than traditional alternatives."
- **Funnel (family):** "Find a carer" CTA → care-request form (needs, specialist skills, daily-life details) → "profiles of suitable self-employed carers within 24 hours" → family can "chat, call, or video" shortlisted carers → confirmation call → care starts, managed via **MyElder** account ([elder.org/live-in-care](https://www.elder.org/live-in-care/), [elder.org/how-elder-works](https://www.elder.org/how-elder-works/)). Family chooses the carer — choice is the core UX differentiator vs managed agencies that assign staff.
- **Product features:** MyElder portal (carer updates, schedule/break planning, care information hub), matching/shortlisting engine, in-platform chat/video pre-hire, payment processing, respite-cover rebooking. Carer side: placement feed ("hub") with per-placement rates, newsfeed-based support.
- **Trust signals:** Trustpilot 4.7 badge; "9 out of 10 customers are very satisfied"; "Only 2% of applicants… successful"; "90% of our customers find their ideal carer the first time"; "5,000+ families connected"; "12 million+ hours of care… 500+ towns and cities"; £5M public liability insurance; awards wall; named-customer testimonials; explicit CQC-status disclosure.
- **Risk-reversal stack (notable):** 14-day trial, no deposit, no joining/exit fees, no bank-holiday surcharge, 2-week notice, free replacement/respite cover. They sell the *absence of lock-in* hard.

## 6. Regulatory stance

- Introduction agency under the Health & Social Care Act 2008 — **not CQC-registered/inspected**, and they disclose this in plain sight on the homepage and service pages rather than burying it ([elder.org](https://www.elder.org/)).
- They convert the regulatory gap into a price story ("35% cheaper than traditional alternatives") and compensate with private trust infrastructure: 2% acceptance, vetting, insurance, Trustpilot.
- Third-party comparisons note the trade-off explicitly: introductory agencies "give you more choice and sometimes… lower cost, but families take on more responsibility… because these services aren't CQC-regulated" ([uniquecare.co.uk comparison](https://www.uniquecare.co.uk/best-live-in-care-companies-compared/)).
- Sector-level risk: HMRC employment-status scrutiny of "self-employed" live-in carers placed via introducers (see §4). Elder setting placement rates itself arguably weakens the self-employment case (inference — no enforcement action against Elder found).

## 8. Strengths, weaknesses, lessons for CareMatch

**Strengths**
- Best-in-category trust packaging: 2% acceptance rate, insurance, trial period, no-lock-in guarantees, Trustpilot 4.7 — makes an unregulated model feel safe.
- Published prices + all-inclusive weekly rate = simpler buying decision than agency quotes.
- Massive funding-advice SEO moat capturing families at the CHC/hospital-discharge moment.
- Family chooses the carer (chat/video pre-hire) — genuine marketplace UX advantage over managed agencies.

**Weaknesses**
- **The take rate is the product's Achilles heel:** ~40% inferred spread angers carers ("greediest commissions"), caps carer quality/retention, and invites undercutting by lower-take marketplaces (Curam at 12.5%+VAT, per [curamcare.com](https://www.curamcare.com/blogs/working-as-a-self-employed-carer)).
- Revenue declining, negative equity, cash burn — the concierge layer at that take rate has not produced a self-sustaining business even at ~£25M+ inferred GMV.
- Opaque fee split (neither side sees the spread) is increasingly out of step with transparent competitors and creates reputational risk.
- Elder setting rates while claiming carers are self-employed is a latent regulatory/tax exposure.
- Live-in-only economics: high AOV but tiny addressable pool vs hourly visiting care; their visiting-care offer looks underdeveloped.

**Lessons for CareMatch**
1. **Disclose the CQC introduction-agency status prominently and weaponise it as a price story** ("~35% cheaper") — Elder proves plain-sight disclosure doesn't hurt conversion when wrapped in private trust signals (vetting %, insurance, reviews, trial).
2. **Publish prices and kill lock-in.** "From £X/week, no joining fee, no deposit, 14-day trial, 2-week notice" is Elder's highest-leverage conversion stack and cheap for a self-serve platform to copy.
3. **Don't copy the 40% take.** Elder's spread funds a concierge layer yet the company still shrinks and burns cash, while carers publicly resent the cut. A transparent, low-take (10-15%) model with carer-set rates is both the economic and regulatory high ground for a true self-serve introducer — let Elder own the expensive middle.
4. **Build the funding-navigation content moat early:** CHC, local-authority funding, hospital discharge, "cost of live-in care" — these queries are where Elder mints demand. This is replicable with programmatic + editorial SEO before paid channels.
5. **Keep carers price-setting and genuinely self-employed.** It differentiates against Elder on carer earnings (recruitment message: "keep more of what families pay"), and it keeps the employment-status/IR35 risk profile clean — Elder's rate-setting is the precedent to avoid.
