# UK Care Marketplace — Competitive Landscape Synthesis

## How to use this research

This file is the decision-oriented synthesis; each competitor has a full per-company report (business model, real pricing, acquisition channels, supply side, regulatory stance, financials, lessons) in this directory — see the [Index](#index) at the bottom for one-line descriptions and links. All claims below are sourced from those reports; anything the underlying report flagged as inferred/uncertain is flagged here too.

## Landscape map

Four strategic positions, defined by (a) who holds the care relationship and (b) whether the platform touches the transaction:

| Category | Who | Position |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct marketplaces** (CareMatch's category) | [Curam](curam.md), [PrimeCarers](primecarers.md), [TrustonTap](trustontap.md), [HomeTouch](hometouch.md), [Country Cousins](country-cousins.md), [Care.com UK](care-com-uk.md) | Introduce families to self-employed carers; family holds the contract; platform monetises the match and (usually) the payment flow. Not CQC-registered (introduction-agency exemption), though Curam and HomeTouch bolt on regulated managed arms. |
| **Managed / hybrid providers** | [Elder](elder.md), [Cera](cera.md), [Helping Hands](helping-hands.md) | Own or heavily manage the care delivery. Cera and Helping Hands employ carers and are CQC-registered; Elder is legally an introducer but operates (and prices) like a managed concierge. These set the price umbrella marketplaces undercut. |
| **Directories / aggregators** | [Lottie](lottie.md), [Autumna](autumna.md), [homecare.co.uk](homecare-co-uk.md) | List *regulated providers* (never individual carers), free for families, monetise the supply side (leads or subscriptions). No transaction layer. They own the top-of-funnel SEO real estate. |
| **Defunct lessons** | [SuperCarers](supercarers.md) (closest analogue to CareMatch; sold to Home Instead 2020, shut), [Care Sourcer](care-sourcer.md) (free agency-matcher; assets sold to KareHero 2023, shell dissolved 2025) | The two documented failure modes: liquidity-starved carer marketplace, and traffic-rich matcher that never owned the transaction. |

Key structural fact: **nobody currently combines (1) self-employed-carer marketplace, (2) genuine self-serve product, (3) transparent pricing, and (4) hourly care at scale.** Curam is closest and is the benchmark to beat.

## Pricing landscape

**Family-side hourly (visiting care):**
- Self-employed carer raw rates: **£13–17/hr** (Care.com average £14.95).
- Marketplace all-in prices: **£18–26/hr** (PrimeCarers £19.47 avg, Curam £20.50 avg +12%, TrustonTap £26 avg).
- Managed agencies: **£23–38/hr** (Cera ~£23.30, Lottie's quoted market avg £28, Helping Hands £32.40 + £4.50/visit; homecare.co.uk market data £26–38).
- **The gap:** a ~£8–15/hr spread between marketplace and managed pricing. Every marketplace's core pitch is "25–50% cheaper than agencies" (TrustonTap 25%, Elder 35%, PrimeCarers 30–50%) — and it's arithmetically true.

**Family-side live-in (weekly):**
- Marketplaces: £1,200/wk (Curam, TrustonTap) — £1,400/wk (HomeTouch intro); PrimeCarers ~£1,079/wk (£154.15/day). SuperCarers was £795/wk in 2019 money.
- Managed/concierge: Elder £1,210+, HomeTouch regulated £1,500+, Helping Hands £1,675+, market range £900–2,000 (homecare.co.uk).
- Live-in is the margin anchor: high ticket, and where HomeTouch retreated to when hourly economics failed.

**Take-rate clusters (the central pricing decision):**
- **~12.5–15%** — carer-side single commission (PrimeCarers live-in/6h+, Curam carer side). The "lowest fees" marketing position.
- **~23–24% blended** — the proven dual-sided norm: Curam (15% carer + 12% client), TrustonTap (18% carer + 5% family), Country Cousins (~24% effective, all family-side). SuperCarers charged 25%/20% and it wasn't the fee that killed them.
- **~30–40%+** — HomeTouch (20–30%) and Elder (~35–40% inferred spread): funds concierge ops but breeds carer resentment and, in Elder's case, a shrinking loss-making business.
- **55–60% gross** — employed-model agencies (Helping Hands): the umbrella everyone undercuts.
- **Non-commission models:** Care.com's £25–30/mo family subscription (proves willingness-to-pay for access alone, and its 73% one-star reviews prove how not to run it); directory flat fees of £33–75/mo (Autumna) anchor what supply will pay for "marketing" untied to outcomes.

**Where the gaps are:** (1) transparent all-in hourly pricing at 15–20% total take with a real self-serve product — Curam has fee opacity complaints, PrimeCarers charges 20% exactly where hourly volume sits (<6h visits); (2) the sub-£1,400/wk self-serve live-in segment HomeTouch abandoned; (3) short visits — SuperCarers' 3h minimum and TrustonTap's 4h/wk minimum leave the pop-in segment to regulated agencies (though margin there is thin).

## Client-acquisition playbooks

Channels that recur across winners, ranked by prevalence:

1. **Programmatic local SEO — universal among survivors.** PrimeCarers: ~2,332 `/carers-{town}` pages with real local average prices, live carer cards, named local client managers with Calendly links. Country Cousins: 2,000–3,000+ town/village pages. HomeTouch: ~120 county live-in pages. Helping Hands: 155+ branch × service × cost pages. Care.com: thousands of category × town pages showing live supply counts. Curam: locations map + condition pages + cost calculator. The pattern that works is **data-rich templates** (real prices, real profiles, local FAQs), not thin doorway pages. Caveat from the graveyard: SuperCarers built 40 city pages while serving hourly care only in London — programmatic SEO ahead of supply just generates leads you can't serve.
2. **Cost-transparency / funding content — the highest-intent keyword cluster.** "Cost of live-in care 2026", "how much do carers cost", Attendance Allowance / NHS Continuing Healthcare / direct-payments guides. Elder built its moat intercepting families at the funding-confusion and hospital-discharge moments; Lottie and Autumna publish market-price data (Autumna's from its own listings dataset) as SEO/PR; Country Cousins' blog leaks the prices its sales pages hide. Managed incumbents can't publish honest cost-comparison maths without exposing their margin — a structural content advantage for marketplaces.
3. **Council / NHS / B2B partnerships — the scale engine for the category leader.** Curam: 25+ direct local authorities plus the Capita partnership reaching 100+ councils' Direct Payment users, NHS discharge teams, case managers, charities (Alzheimer's Society, Parkinson's UK). Cera: 150+ councils, 2/3 of NHS ICSs (its whole business). HomeTouch: dedicated partnerships team for ICBs/discharge/case managers ("care in 24–72 hours"). TrustonTap and Autumna: free listings in council "Live Well"-style directories, reaching social workers. Counterweight: Care Sourcer's £757k NHS contract shows NHS procurement is real money but a brutal, slow sales motion — treat as year-2+ upside, not launch GTM.
4. **Directories as a channel.** homecare.co.uk's own claim: 60% of enquirers find care within 2 months — warm leads at £414+VAT/yr. HomeTouch, TrustonTap and others list on TrustedCare, liveincare.org.uk, agespace. Whether non-CQC introducers qualify for homecare.co.uk listing is uncertain — verify.
5. **Human conversion layer on top of self-serve.** Every converter has one: PrimeCarers' named local client managers, Curam's prominent phone line, Lottie's Care Experts (which make leads "qualified" and billable), Autumna's 7-day advice line + 24h shortlist SLA. Pure self-serve with no human fallback appears nowhere among winners; pure phone-only (Country Cousins, Helping Hands, Cera) is the incumbent weakness to attack.
6. **Reviews as infrastructure.** Trustpilot farming (Curam ~600 reviews, Elder 750+, Helping Hands 5,050+); homecare.co.uk's lawyer-overseen, provider-can't-block verification process is the credibility template. Thin review bases (PrimeCarers ~46, Country Cousins ~120) visibly undermine scale claims.
7. **Referral/benefit channels (secondary):** SuperCarers' Vitality insurer benefit and My Family Care employer channel; Lottie's Seniorcare and Autumna's Vivup employee-benefit schemes; KareHero (Care Sourcer's buyer) is entirely employer-paid — the proven B2B2C fallback if consumer monetisation stalls. Speed promises ("care in 24–48h" — Country Cousins, Cera, Helping Hands, HomeTouch) recur as the crisis/discharge conversion hook. No competitor shows evidence of heavy paid-search dependence (mostly unverified either way).

## Supply-side patterns

**Vetting norms (the trust bar CareMatch must meet or beat):**
- Standard stack across serious marketplaces: **enhanced DBS (ideally on the update service) + ID verification (Yoti/biometric) + right-to-work + 2 references + experience minimum + interview.** Experience minimums: 6 months (HomeTouch, Country Cousins), 1 year (PrimeCarers, SuperCarers), 2 years + Care Certificate (Curam — the highest bar).
- **Selectivity is marketed as a conversion asset, not overhead:** Curam "1 in 7", TrustonTap ~5%, HomeTouch "<5%, 46-point vetting", Elder "2%", SuperCarers 5%, Helping Hands "2%". Publish the number.
- Care.com is the control group: zero vetting → huge supply, 2.3/5 trust, terms that contradict the "trusted" headline.
- Recurring gap even among vetters: vetting is **opaque to families at decision time** (Country Cousins' anonymised CVs, TrustonTap's inaccurate profiles). Showing verified evidence on the profile is an open differentiator.

**Carer economics (what attracts supply):**
- Agency-employed baseline: **£11–14/hr** (Helping Hands ~£11–13, Cera £12.30–13.85) with weak conditions — the pool to recruit from.
- Marketplace take-home: **£17–22/hr** (Curam claims £17.85/hr net avg, "25% more than agency"; TrustonTap "up to £22/hr take-home, 40% more than agency"). Live-in: £700–900/wk self-employed vs ≤£675/wk employed (Helping Hands' own FAQ concedes the gap).
- What carers keep: 75–87.5% of their self-set rate on marketplaces vs ~40% of family spend at managed agencies — "keep more of what families pay" is the universal, evidence-backed recruitment message.
- **Carrots that work:** free joining (universal among winners; only Care.com upsells carers), carer-set rates, weekly pay (PrimeCarers, TrustonTap, HomeTouch; Curam ~2–3 days), platform-provided insurance (Curam £1M+£1M auto-activated, tied to platform hours — doubles as anti-disintermediation), free training (HomeTouch, Helping Hands), referral bonuses (£250 Country Cousins), low-friction onboarding (PrimeCarers: 10-min signup + 20-min interview vs Curam's app-mandatory Care-Certificate-prerequisite funnel that has 50k stuck in pipeline vs 11k approved).
- **What repels supply:** opaque/high takes (Elder's ~40% spread → "greediest commissions"), platform-set rates while claiming self-employment (Elder — also an IR35/employment-status exposure flagged in sector commentary).

**Disintermediation — the model's existential leak.** Every payment-intermediating marketplace enforces: cash bans + circumvention fees (Curam £1,000; PrimeCarers £2,500 + £5,000 liquidated damages; HomeTouch £5,000 exit fee). Country Cousins doesn't intermediate payments at all and instead charges an ongoing family-side fee — which reviews suggest invites churn once family and carer know each other. Consensus lesson: pair one modest contractual stick with carrots that make staying rational — platform-hours-only insurance, escrow/fast payout, replacement-carer access, dispute support, care records.

## Failure lessons

**SuperCarers (closest precedent to CareMatch; sold to Home Instead 2020, marketplace shut within months):**
1. **Liquidity never generalised beyond London** — 4+ years in, hourly care was still London-only while a 40-city SEO footprint harvested demand they had to hand to regulated agencies as leads.
2. **Marketplace margins with agency cost structure** — phone advisory teams, face-to-face group interviews, 5% acceptance: every human step improved trust and destroyed unit economics.
3. **3-hour minimum visits** excluded the highest-frequency demand segment.
4. ~£5M was not enough to solve two-sided liquidity + trust + death/care-home churn; COVID closed the funding window. Solvent asset sale, not a crash — but not a venture return.
5. Bonus flag: SuperCarers ran a Vitality-branded service literally named **"CareMatch"** (~2019–20) — check trademark/passing-off exposure before committing to the brand.

**Care Sourcer (assets sold to KareHero 2023; shell dissolved 2025):**
1. **Never owned the transaction** — free matching to third-party agencies, all money off-platform; revenue collapsed to lead fees and lumpy partnerships.
2. **Charged the side that felt no scarcity** — in a supply-constrained care market, agencies with full rosters don't buy leads.
3. **Traffic ≠ business** — 2nd-most-visited UK elderly-care site, record £8.5M Scottish Series A, and the only durable asset was the SEO funnel (which is what KareHero bought). Its strategic investor (L&G) built its own concierge in-house.

**What survivors do differently:** intermediate payments and take a commission (Curam, PrimeCarers, TrustonTap); keep the ops layer lean/self-serve or regional-dense (PrimeCarers bootstrapped ~23 staff; TrustonTap 3-person HQ); or escape upmarket to high-ticket live-in (HomeTouch — survival, not success); or don't touch the transaction at all but monetise supply cheaply at scale with 15–30 years of domain authority (homecare.co.uk, Autumna). The middle position — high-touch, high-take, VC-fuelled national marketplace — is where Elder is currently shrinking and where SuperCarers died.

## Strategic implications for CareMatch

1. **Revenue model: dual-sided commission at ~20–24% blended, intermediating payments, is the field-tested optimum.** Curam (15% carer + 12% client), TrustonTap (18% + 5%) and SuperCarers (25%) all prove it clears; PrimeCarers proves 12.5–20% carer-side-only also works and keeps family pricing simple. The tradeoff triangle: **carer-side commission** (simple family price, but carers compare takes — must stay ≤20% to recruit), **family-side fees** (Country Cousins gets ~24% without touching carers, but with no payment rails it invites disintermediation), **family subscription** (Care.com's £25–30/mo proves willingness-to-pay but pay-before-value drove 73% one-star reviews). Avoid the two extremes with evidence attached: Elder's ~40% opaque spread (shrinking, resented) and Care Sourcer's 0% (dead). Given Stripe-first plumbing is already planned, commission-on-intermediated-payments is the natural fit — Care Sourcer is the proof that not owning the transaction is fatal.
2. **Beat PrimeCarers where the volume is: the hourly tier.** Their "lowest fees" claim is only true for live-in (12.5%); short visits pay 20%. A flat ≤15% total take on hourly care is a truthful "lowest fees" wedge in the highest-frequency segment — but note HomeTouch's warning that pure hourly introduction produced too little per-client margin for them; model CAC against churn-adjusted per-client margin (client lifetimes in this category end by death/hospitalisation/care-home admission) and keep live-in in the mix as the margin anchor.
3. **Launch hyperlocal; prove density before building the national SEO footprint.** TrustonTap's whole trust story (24–48h match, same carer) rests on ~200 carers in one county; SuperCarers died writing national SEO cheques London-only supply couldn't cash. Pick one region, hit meaningful carer density, then expand pages with supply — or consciously monetise out-of-area demand as agency referrals (SuperCarers ran this as a real revenue stream).
4. **Be radically price-transparent — publish the rate card, the commission, and a cost calculator.** Every phone-led incumbent hides pricing (Country Cousins "on request", Cera, Helping Hands' carers' rates) and every marketplace that publishes (Curam calculator, TrustonTap exact bands, Elder "from £1,210/no joining fee") converts on it. Show the all-in family price on every profile — Curam's fee-surprise complaints (12% surfacing at invoice) are the specific mistake to avoid. Managed incumbents structurally cannot match this without exposing 50%+ gross takes.
5. **Make vetting selective, quantified, and visible on the profile.** Match the category bar (enhanced DBS on update service, ID verification, 2 references, experience minimum, interview) and publish the acceptance rate — but keep onboarding fast (PrimeCarers' 10-min/20-min funnel vs Curam's app+Care-Certificate throttle that leaves 50k in pipeline). The open gap: show families the *evidence* (verified DBS status, named checks, training badges) at decision time — every incumbent keeps it opaque, and it's their most common complaint.
6. **Recruit carers on the pay gap with concrete arithmetic.** "Families pay Helping Hands £32.40/hr; the carer sees ~£11–13. On CareMatch you set your rate and keep ~85%." Cera pays £12.30–13.85/hr and needed an AI agent to keep its funnel full — the employed-agency workforce is the recruitment pool and the comparison is devastating. Keep carers genuinely price-setting: Elder's platform-set rates are both the carer-resentment driver and the IR35/employment-status precedent to avoid.
7. **Build the anti-disintermediation bundle into v1:** payments locked in-platform (escrow-style, fast carer payout), insurance covering platform-booked hours only, contracts/invoicing tooling, replacement-carer access, plus one modest circumvention fee (~£1,000, Curam-level — PrimeCarers' £2,500/£5,000 and HomeTouch's £5,000 read hostile and surface in reviews). Product value first, penalty second.
8. **Attack the reliability gap — the introduction model's soft underbelly.** The worst reviews of every introducer are continuity failures (Curam "5 carers in 5 months"; TrustonTap short-notice cancellations) and it's the exact objection script Helping Hands' anti-introducer marketing runs (who covers sickness? who finds a replacement? who monitors?). Ship features that map 1:1 to those objections: backup-carer pools (Curam's "Micro Teams" is a half-step), visit confirmations/check-ins, a care journal (HomeTouch proves clinical-feeling visibility can ship without CQC scope), standardised weekly pricing.
9. **Draw the CQC line in writing, candidly, and get legal sign-off.** The convergent pattern (SuperCarers wrote it, Curam/Elder/HomeTouch copied): plain footer statement of introduction-agency status, an honest FAQ on what the platform cannot do (no rota management, no emergency cover, no care direction), and signposting to regulated providers for managed care. HomeTouch's explicit "what we cannot do" list and Country Cousins' intro-vs-managed comparison table show candour converts. Watch the boundary if adding a paid "managed-lite" tier (PrimeCarers' +5%/+10% coordination tiers sit untested against CQC scope).
10. **SEO strategy: town × service pages with real data + cost/funding content, sequenced behind supply.** The conversion-grade template is PrimeCarers' (local average prices, live carer cards, human contact) not thin doorway pages; the highest-intent cluster is cost/funding (CHC, Attendance Allowance, direct payments, "cost of live-in care") where Elder mints demand. Add council directory listings (free, reaches social workers — TrustonTap/Autumna playbook) and a homecare.co.uk presence (~£414/yr, eligibility for introducers uncertain — verify) from day one; treat NHS discharge/ICB partnerships as the year-2 scale engine (Curam's Capita/100-council route is the ceiling; Care Sourcer's contract-then-quiet arc is the floor).
11. **Add a thin human layer, not a concierge org.** Winners bolt named humans onto self-serve funnels (PrimeCarers' local client managers, Autumna's 24h shortlist SLA, Lottie's qualifying Care Experts); losers built call-centre matching into the cost base (SuperCarers, and Elder's economics today). A phone number, callback option, and a 24h shortlist promise are cheap; keep matching itself self-serve.
12. **Two hedges worth pre-architecting:** (a) an org-account layer so a council Direct-Payments team or case manager can post jobs and get consolidated billing — same product, bulk demand, Curam's proven scale channel; (b) the B2B2C fallback — employer/insurer-paid care navigation is the model that keeps getting funded (KareHero, Lottie Seniorcare, Autumna's Vivup channel, SuperCarers' Vitality deal) if direct consumer monetisation disappoints.
